**Gender pay gap — Italy vs EU**

Sources: Eurostat "Gender pay gap statistics" (data 2020, published 2022): `https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/statistics-explained/index.php?title=Gender_pay_gap_statistics` — Eurostat Statistics Explained PDF (2024 data)

- Italy unadjusted gender pay gap 2020: 4.2% — one of the lowest in the EU alongside Luxembourg (0.7%), Romania (2.4%), Slovenia (3.1%)
- EU average unadjusted gender pay gap 2020: 13.0%
- EU average unadjusted gender pay gap 2024: 11.1%
- Italy data for 2024 not confirmed in this session — the 4.2% figure refers specifically to 2020

**Critical interpretive note on Italy's apparently low gender pay gap (Eurostat confirmed):**
The low Italian figure is distorted by selection effects. Women engaging in the Italian labour market have comparatively higher skills and education levels than men — the women most penalized by the labour market have simply exited it. ILO data confirms the distortion: Italy's hourly gender pay gap is approximately 2.2% but the monthly gap is 16.7%, reflecting the structural concentration of women in part-time and lower-hours contracts.

**Motherhood penalty — Italy specific**

Primary source: Casarico & Lattanzio (2023), "The child penalty in Italy", *Journal of Population Economics* 36(3), pp. 1489–1511
Direct link: `https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00148-023-00937-1`
Dataset: employer-employee matched data Italy 1985–2018
Summary by same authors on CEPR VoxEU: `https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/tackling-gender-gaps-italian-labour-market-evidence-and-policy-implications`

**Key findings — verified and correctly attributed:**

- Child penalty on annual earnings: 52 log points (paper original metric) — translated by CEPR as "approximately 40% less earnings than non-mothers at 15 years after birth" — translated by IMF Country Report 2024 as "approximately 50% relative to pre-birth earnings." All three figures derive from the same paper but measure different things: the 52 log points is the econometric measure; the 40% is a cross-sectional comparison at 15 years; the 50% is a longitudinal comparison relative to pre-birth baseline. The CEPR figure (40%) is correctly attributed to the CEPR summary, not to the paper directly.
- The probability for employed women to be non-employed in the two years following maternity doubles that of women without children — verified from CEPR
- This employment gap persists and remains significant even 15 years after childbirth — verified from CEPR
- For non-employed women, the likelihood of finding a job significantly decreases after birth of a child and remains lower for at least five years — verified from CEPR
- Child penalties are larger in regions with more traditional gender norms — North/South divide documented: 65% of women combine work with motherhood in the North vs 45% in the South (IMF Country Report 2024)
- Eliminating the existing child penalty among new mothers would increase the female employment rate by 6.5 percentage points by 2040 (De Philippis & Lo Bello 2023, cited in CEPR)

**Childcare availability as structural constraint**

Source: IMF Italy Country Report 2024: `https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/cr/2024/english/1itaea2024002.pdf`

- Monthly childcare cost in Italy: approximately 300–400 euros
- Average monthly family allowance (assegno unico): approximately 160 euros
- Net childcare cost after subsidy exceeds the allowance by 140–240 euros per month — a structural financial disincentive for second earners
- Italy's childcare enrollment rate for 0–2 year-olds: approximately 26% vs EU average 33% — figure plausible and consistent with OECD Family Database but requires direct ISTAT/OECD verification as primary source

**International framework — motherhood penalty comparative**

Sources: Journal of Family and Economic Issues, Springer (2026): `https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10834-025-10072-6` — ScienceDirect meta-analysis (2020): `https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0049089X20300144`

- Each additional child reduces a mother's wage by approximately 4–10% on average across countries (England et al. 2016)
- Meta-analysis of 208 studies: average motherhood wage gap approximately 3.6–3.8%
- The penalty is smallest in Nordic countries and France, where public policies actively support work-family reconciliation
- The penalty is largest in post-socialist Central and Eastern European countries
- The motherhood penalty stands in contrast to the documented "fatherhood premium" — fathers are often perceived as more reliable and rewarded with career advancement
