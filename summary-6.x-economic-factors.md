# Summary: Economic Factors (6.x)

## GDP-TFR Chronology
- 1964: TFR 2.66 during economic miracle | 1975: ~2.3 during oil crisis
- 1983: ~1.5 during recession | 1988: ~1.36 during "second economic miracle" (GDP growth ~3-4%)
- 1995: 1.19 historic low during stability | 2008: 1.44 moderate growth
- **Paradox**: Sharpest TFR drop (1983-1995) occurred during sustained prosperity (1984-1990: 26 quarters growth, +21.4% GDP)
- Economic prosperity coincided with fertility decline - contradicts economic causation hypothesis

## Theoretical Framework
- New Home Economics: Wealthier parents invest more in child "quality" → fewer children
- Opportunity cost: Rising wages make childcare time more expensive
- International pattern: Fertility declines as nations grow richer, not poorer

## Regional Evidence: South Inverts Economic Argument
- North/Central: Higher unemployment negatively correlated with fertility
- South: Effect reversed - higher unemployment with higher fertility
- Historical: South with highest unemployment maintained higher fertility than wealthy North
- Female labor participation: Negative correlation 1960s → positive correlation 1990s

## TFR Partial Recovery (1995-2008)
- TFR rose 1.19→1.44 during stable years
- Foreign-born women drove Northern Italy recovery disproportionately
- Native population fertility did not recover structurally
- Post-2008: TFR resumed decline even before austerity

## Sardinia Case
- 1960: Highest TFR Italy (3.5) despite economic underdevelopment
- 2000: Lowest TFR Italy (1.04)
- No exceptional economic crisis; variable was cultural (urbanization, secularization, female education, individualism)

## Speed of Modernization (Goldin)
- Group 1 (steady growth): Denmark, France, Germany, Sweden, UK, USA - TFR above 1.5
- Group 2 (rapid post-1950): Greece, Italy, Japan, South Korea, Portugal, Spain - TFR below 1.3
- Mechanism: Rapid modernization creates gender conflict
- Unpaid work gap: Group 2 shows 2.5+ hours daily vs 0.8-1.7 Group 1

## Economic Justification
- Economic constraints provide socially legitimate justification for culturally-rooted choices
- Real costs amplified into moral veto - "perpetual waiting for better moment"
- Nordic counterexample: High costs but high fertility due to institutional support
- Economic instability amplifies underlying cultural tendencies rather than causing decline

## Key Insights
- Economic factors are amplifier, not cause of fertility decline
- Speed of cultural change (not poverty) produces lowest-low fertility
- Institutional failure is consequence of cultural/political choices
- Economic solutions alone insufficient; institutional investment essential
