## Cultural Narratives: Psychological Impact of Catastrophic Demographic Discourse on Reproductive Agency

- Analyze the media monitoring methodology developed by the Osservatorio di Pavia for understanding how discourse shapes social reality. Since 1994, the Osservatorio di Pavia has conducted systematic media monitoring activities analyzing political communication in Italian mass media in partnership with RAI, the Italian public service broadcaster. Their methodological framework examines how media contribute significantly to the construction of social reality and public opinion through selecting issues of interest and framing them along specific perspectives. This analytical framework can be applied to demographic discourse, where catastrophic framing of fertility decline may generate collective anxiety that influences individual reproductive choices.

- Document how media framing can generate collective anxiety and social closure through the Osservatorio di Pavia's research on migration and multiculturalism. Their analysis demonstrates that media discourse sometimes frames diversity in stereotyped ways or endorses discrimination targeting minorities, contributing to a climate of public fear and social closure. This finding establishes a general mechanism by which media discourse—including demographic discourse—can shape public sentiment and potentially influence life choices by amplifying perceived threats and uncertainty.

- Examine the structural barriers documented in the Istituto Toniolo's decade of youth research (2013–2023) that translate uncertainty into delayed life transitions. The *Rapporto Giovani* series systematically analyzes continuity and transformations in the condition of youth on structural themes: family, transition to adulthood, school, mobility, work, NEET phenomenon, poverty and inequality. These structural factors create conditions where young people experience prolonged economic dependence and delayed milestones, forming the material basis upon which demographic discourse acts.

- Quantify the gap between institutional trust and youth agency as a potential mediator of demographic fatalism. A 2024 national survey of 3,427 adolescents (aged 13–19) conducted by Laboratorio adolescenti and the Iard research institute reveals a profound crisis of institutional trust. Trust in the political class stands at approximately 3%, while trust in teachers has declined from around 70% to 48% over ten years, and trust in priests has fallen from 52% to 29%. The only figures adolescents fully trust are parents (90%) and friends (86%). As Maurizio Tucci, president of Laboratorio adolescenza, notes: trusting only family and friends represents discomfort in facing the outside world. This retreat from institutional trust may create fertile ground for demographic fatalism: when young people perceive that broader social institutions are indifferent or incapable of shaping a stable future, the motivation to invest in long-term projects such as parenthood may diminish.

- Analyze the geography of hope and disillusionment among Italian youth as revealed by the 2025 Ipsos-Toniolo survey on young adults. Conducted on a representative sample of 2,001 young Italians aged 18–34 between February 17 and March 3, 2025, the survey reveals significant regional variation in hope. In Northeast Italy—often associated with economic dynamism—approximately 44% of young people declare themselves "very or extremely hopeful" about the future. Rates are higher in the Northwest (around 48%), the South (around 46%), and Central Italy (around 45%). Women across all regions report lower levels of hope than men. These findings suggest that catastrophic demographic discourse may interact with regional economic conditions to shape psychological resources for family formation.

- Identify the structural determinants of hope that may counteract demographic fatalism. The same survey identifies work as a critical factor: workers demonstrate significantly higher average scores than non-workers in personal mastery, social support, and spirituality components of the Integrated Hope Scale. As one young chef interviewed for the survey stated: "Work gives you a place. It makes you feel useful. It gives you hope." Conversely, students report lower levels of hope. This finding suggests that the absence of stable employment—a key theme in demographic discourse—directly impacts the psychological resources needed to contemplate parenthood.

- Explore the relationship between spirituality, hope, and demographic fatalism. The Integrated Hope Scale, which measures hope across four components—personal mastery, social support, trust, and spirituality—identifies spirituality as the most fragile component among young Italians. Young people generally do not believe in something greater, do not confess, or attend church. However, those who practice religion solidly or cultivate a form of faith show higher levels of hope. This finding resonates with broader research on secularization and fertility, suggesting that the erosion of transcendent frameworks for meaning-making may leave young people more vulnerable to demographic narratives of inevitable decline.

- Examine the dual faces of hope among Italian youth as documented by the 2025 Ipsos-Toniolo adolescent survey. Conducted on 815 adolescents aged 14–19 between June 30 and July 15, 2025, the research reveals that despite pervasive fears of failure, young people maintain active hope. On the Hope Theory scale (Snyder), adolescents score around 3.6 out of 5 on "pathway" (the ability to imagine alternative paths to achieve goals) and 3.5 on "agency" (motivational strength to pursue them), with younger adolescents (14–16) scoring higher than older peers. As Elena Marta, professor of social and community psychology at the Catholic University, explains: behind fragility there is also a desire for redemption and the future. Hope, supported by the ability to imagine alternative paths and internal motivation, represents the resource on which to work to build effective educational interventions.

- Identify the sources of generational anxiety that demographic discourse may amplify. The same adolescent survey reveals that fear of failure is pervasive, particularly around school—a context where failure is perceived not as a circumscribed event, but as an overall judgment on one's worth. Students report that dismissive comments, humiliating grades, and misplaced jokes become daily violence that leaves deep marks on self-esteem. High school students experience greater pressure than technical or vocational students, with higher expectations and lower tolerance for personal difficulty. This suggests that psychological barriers to family formation may originate earlier in the life course than typically considered, shaping the emotional resources available for future reproductive decisions.

- Analyze the counter-narrative: young people's moral sensitivity and civic engagement. Against stereotypes of disengagement, the 2025 adolescent survey reveals strong commitment to moral values. On the Moral Foundations Relevance scale (1–6), adolescents rank highest on "caring/not causing harm" (around 4.6), "justice and equity" (around 4.6), and "purity and personal integrity" (around 4.5). Girls and younger adolescents (14–16) show the highest sensitivity. The 2025 young adult survey further reveals that when asked "How do you imagine yourself in the future?", many young people responded with civic engagement, the environment, and care for the context in which they live. They dream of being responsible citizens. This tangible hope, made of daily gestures rather than great ideals that does not come from the sky but from relationships, suggests that interventions aimed at restoring hope through employment, social support networks, and civic engagement may counteract the psychological paralysis induced by apocalyptic demographic narratives.
