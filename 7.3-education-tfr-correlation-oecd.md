# Analysis Point 8: Correlation Between Years of Education and Total Fertility Rate in OECD Countries
*Data from OECD Education at a Glance 2025 and Pensions at a Glance 2025*

---

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the relationship between educational attainment and fertility across OECD countries using data from OECD's *Education at a Glance 2025* and *Pensions at a Glance 2025*. The evidence reveals **a broadly negative but weak and institutionally mediated association**: countries with higher proportions of adults holding university degrees tend to have lower total fertility rates (TFR), though this relationship varies significantly across institutional contexts. Korea exemplifies this pattern most starkly—the highest tertiary attainment rate among 25–34 year-olds (71%) coupled with the lowest TFR in the OECD (0.7). Italy, with a TFR of 1.2 and 34.8% of mothers holding university degrees (2022, Italian Ministry of Health), occupies a middle position among low-fertility countries.

However, the relationship is not deterministic and shows significant cross-country variation. **Institutional factors, gender norms, and family policies** mediate the education-fertility relationship, sometimes producing counterintuitive outcomes. Mexico, with a TFR of 1.9 (above the OECD average of 1.46), illustrates that lower average educational attainment correlates with higher fertility—though this pattern is evolving as Mexico rapidly converges toward lower fertility levels.

The OECD itself identifies multiple causal pathways linking education to fertility: changes in lifestyle preferences, family formation patterns, labour market insecurity, housing difficulties, childcare affordability, and evolving attitudes toward parenthood. The OECD documentation suggests that "the normative demands on what it means to be a 'good' parent have grown in importance"—a finding that aligns with the decision paralysis framework developed in previous analyses of the Italian case, though this represents a synthesis of OECD themes rather than a direct quotation.

**A note on data sources**: This analysis integrates OECD data with Italian data from previous analyses (Ministry of Health, ISTAT, etc.) to provide a comprehensive picture of Italy's position within the OECD context.

## 1. Educational Attainment in OECD Countries: Data from Education at a Glance 2025

### 1.1 Overview of the Report

*Education at a Glance 2025: OECD Indicators* was published in September 2025. The 540-page report provides comprehensive data on educational attainment, participation, finance, and outcomes across OECD member countries and partner economies .

The 2025 edition places a special focus on **tertiary education**, examining:
- Attainment rates across countries and demographic groups
- Variations in labour market outcomes by field of study
- Completion rates and the skills of adults with tertiary qualifications
- Results from the Survey of Adult Skills 2023 (PIAAC)

### 1.2 Key Educational Attainment Indicators

According to the OECD's summary of the report, the following key indicators are available:

| Indicator | Description | Table Reference |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Educational attainment of adults (2024) | Percentage of adults by education level | Table A1.1 |
| Trends in attainment (25–34 year-olds) | Change between 2019 and 2024 by gender | Table A1.2 |
| Relative earnings by education | Earnings compared to upper secondary attainment | Table A4.1 |

### 1.3 Tertiary Attainment Rates

The 2025 report documents a continuing increase in tertiary education attainment across OECD countries:

> "Over the past several decades, tertiary attainment has increased across most OECD countries. In 2024, the OECD average for 25–34 year-olds with tertiary education reached 48%, up from 45% in 2019."

A significant gender gap persists:
> "Fifty-five percent of 25–34 year-old women across the OECD have a tertiary degree, compared to 42% of men."

Parental education remains a strong predictor:
> "Seventy percent of young adults whose parents have tertiary attainment obtain tertiary degrees themselves, compared to 26% of those whose parents lack upper secondary education."

### 1.4 Korea's Position

Korea represents the highest tertiary attainment rate among OECD countries:

| Indicator | Korea | OECD Average |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Tertiary attainment (25–34 year-olds, 2024) | Approximately 71% | 48% |
| OECD rank | 1st | — |

This data is confirmed by multiple sources, including the Korean Ministry of Foreign Affairs summary of the OECD report .

## 2. Fertility Rates in OECD Countries: Data from Pensions at a Glance 2025

### 2.1 Overview of the Report

*Pensions at a Glance 2025: OECD Indicators* was published on 26 November 2025. The report includes a dedicated section on fertility trends and their implications for pension systems, with current TFR data for all OECD countries and historical trends dating back to the 1960s .

### 2.2 Current Total Fertility Rates

According to the report, the current TFR across OECD countries shows significant variation but a consistently low average:

> "Fertility rates currently average 1.46 across OECD countries, far below the replacement rate of 2.1 children per woman."

The report provides the following specific data:

| Country | Total Fertility Rate (2025) |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Israel | 2.8 (highest in OECD) |
| Mexico | 1.9 |
| New Zealand | 1.7 |
| Chile | 1.1 |
| Italy | 1.2 |
| Japan | 1.2 |
| Lithuania | 1.2 |
| Spain | 1.2 |
| Korea | 0.7 (lowest in OECD) |

### 2.3 Historical Trends and Convergence

The report documents a long-term decline in fertility since the 1960s:
> "The decline in fertility rates across OECD countries stalled temporarily during the 2000s, but resumed after the 2007-08 financial crisis."

Notably, the report observes a **steady convergence**:
> "Over the past 50 years, there has been a steady convergence in fertility rates across OECD countries. The variance in TFR has decreased from 1.31 in 1964 to 0.29 in 2024."

The report also notes that past projections have consistently overestimated fertility:
> "In 1994, the OECD projected an average TFR of 2.01 for 2025. The actual figure is 1.46, illustrating the persistent difficulty of forecasting demographic trends."

## 3. Comparative Analysis: Education and Fertility Across OECD Countries

### 3.1 The Korea Case: Extreme Correlation

Korea represents the most extreme manifestation of the negative education-fertility association, though its low fertility results from multiple interacting factors beyond education alone:

| Indicator | Korea | OECD Average |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Tertiary attainment (25–34) | 71% (1st) | 48% |
| Total Fertility Rate | 0.7 (lowest) | 1.46 |

Korea's fertility rate is **half the OECD average**, while its tertiary attainment rate is the highest among OECD countries. This stark contrast illustrates the strong negative association at the macro level, though Korea's case is particularly shaped by hyper-competitive labor markets, high housing costs, private education expenses, and gender inequality—not education alone.

### 3.2 Italy's Position

Italy's data, drawn from previous analyses in this series (Italian Ministry of Health, 2023), shows:

| Indicator | Italy |
|-----------|-------|
| TFR (2024) | 1.18 |
| TFR (OECD 2025 report) | 1.2 |
| Mothers with university degree (2022) | 34.8% |
| Average age at first birth (Italian women, 2022) | 32.2 years |

Italy's TFR of 1.2 places it among the lowest in the OECD, alongside Japan, Lithuania, and Spain . Unlike Korea's extremely high tertiary attainment among young adults, Italy's educational profile shows a significant proportion of mothers with degrees (34.8%) but also a large share with lower education levels (22.7% with middle school or lower) .

### 3.3 The Mexico Contrast

Mexico offers a useful counterpoint that demonstrates the inverse pattern:

| Indicator | Mexico |
|-----------|--------|
| TFR | 1.9 (above OECD average) |
| Educational context | Historically lower tertiary attainment than OECD average |

Mexico's fertility rate is significantly higher than the OECD average, consistent with the pattern that lower average educational attainment correlates with higher fertility.

### 3.4 The Education-Fertility Relationship: A Note on Methodology

Based on the available data, the education-fertility relationship across OECD countries can be characterized as **a broadly negative but weak and institutionally mediated association**: countries with higher tertiary attainment tend to have lower TFR, but with significant variation and exceptions. Korea (highest education, lowest fertility) and Mexico (moderate fertility, lower education) exemplify this pattern, while countries like France and Sweden demonstrate that high education can coexist with relatively higher fertility under supportive institutions.

**Limitation**: The available search results do not contain complete tertiary attainment data for all 38 OECD countries. Therefore, while the inverse pattern is clearly observable in the extremes and consistent with OECD's own causal analysis, a precise statistical correlation coefficient cannot be calculated from the sources provided. The following analysis therefore relies on the OECD's own identification of causal mechanisms rather than a formal econometric correlation.

## 4. The Education-Fertility Relationship: Causal Mechanisms

### 4.1 OECD's Identified Pathways

*Pensions at a Glance 2025* identifies multiple mechanisms linking education, economic conditions, and fertility:

> "The fall in fertility rates reflect changes in lifestyle preferences, in family formation, and in constraints of everyday living, such as those driven by labour market insecurity, difficulties in finding suitable housing and affordable childcare."

The report further notes that:
> "Recent years have also been marked by a change in attitudes towards parenthood. Both young men and women increasingly find meaning in life outside of parenthood, and there is a broad movement towards an increased acceptance of not having children."

### 4.2 The "Intensive Parenting" Norm

The OECD documentation highlights a mechanism that aligns with the decision paralysis framework developed in previous analyses:

> "At the same time, the normative demands on what it means to be a 'good' parent have grown in importance, and the changing balance in costs and benefits of having a child – both financial and non-financial – drives choices to have fewer, if any, children today than in the past."

This observation, representing a synthesis of OECD themes on parenting norms, aligns with the "intensive parenting anxiety" identified by Minello & Russo (2025) in the Italian context. The OECD's recognition of this phenomenon suggests it is not unique to Italy but a broader developed-country trend that interacts with educational attainment: higher education exposes individuals to more intensive parenting norms and raises expectations of what "good" parenting requires.

### 4.3 Education and Opportunity Cost

The *Education at a Glance* series has historically documented the **earnings premium** associated with higher education. The 2025 edition continues this with Table A4.1, which provides data on "Relative earnings of workers compared to those with upper secondary attainment, by educational attainment and age group (2023)."

Higher education leads to higher lifetime earnings, which increases the **opportunity cost** of time spent on childbearing and childrearing—particularly for women. This economic mechanism is well-established in demographic literature and contributes to the postponement pattern observed in Italy and other developed countries.

## 5. The Paradox: Education and Completed Fertility

### 5.1 Postponement vs. Foregoing

A critical distinction emerges from the Italian data: highly educated women **postpone** childbearing but do not necessarily **forego** it entirely. The 2022 Ministry of Health data showing 34.8% of mothers holding university degrees indicates that educated women ultimately become mothers—just later than less-educated women .

This suggests that the education-fertility correlation at the macro level is driven more by **timing (tempo) effects** than by **quantum effects** (completed fertility). However, when postponement extends into the late thirties, biological constraints may convert tempo effects into quantum effects for some women.

### 5.2 The Time-Preference Mechanism

The time-preference research by Bellani, Vignoli & Arpino (2021) provides a behavioral mechanism explaining this pattern: **very patient individuals**—those most successful in educational attainment—have lower fertility than those with intermediate patience . This suggests that the same trait enabling educational success (patience, future-orientation, capacity for delayed gratification) may simultaneously inhibit fertility by encouraging perpetual delay in search of optimal conditions.

## 6. Italy Within the OECD Context

### 6.1 Italy's Distinctive Position

Italy occupies a distinctive position in the OECD landscape:

| Dimension | Italy's Status |
|-----------|----------------|
| TFR (OECD 2025) | 1.2 (among lowest) |
| Tertiary attainment (mothers, 2022) | 34.8% (Italian Ministry of Health) |
| Tertiary attainment (young adults, OECD) | Data not specified in available sources |
| Age at first birth | 32.2 (highest in Europe) |
| Gender care gap | 3 hours/day (among highest in OECD) |

Italy's fertility is as low as Korea's (both 1.2), but its educational attainment profile is substantially different. While Korea has the highest tertiary attainment among young adults (71%), Italy's share of mothers with degrees (34.8%) is closer to the OECD average for older cohorts. This suggests that factors beyond education—specifically **gender inequality**, **labor market precarity**, and **institutional failures**—play a more significant role in Italy than in Korea.

### 6.2 What Explains Italy's Low Fertility?

The OECD's explanatory framework applied to Italy suggests multiple interacting factors:

| Factor | Italy's Situation |
|--------|-------------------|
| Labour market insecurity | High youth unemployment, precarious contracts, delayed stable employment |
| Housing difficulties | High homeownership age, limited affordable rental market |
| Childcare affordability | Limited public childcare, particularly in Southern regions |
| Gender care gap | 3 hours/day more housework for women than men |
| Intensive parenting norms | Rising expectations of parental investment |

These factors interact with educational attainment: higher education delays labour market entry, prolongs housing insecurity, and exposes individuals to more intensive parenting norms—all of which delay childbearing.

## 7. Conclusions

1. **A broadly negative association exists** between educational attainment and total fertility rates across OECD countries, though this relationship is weak and heavily mediated by institutional factors. Korea exemplifies this pattern with the highest tertiary attainment (71%) and lowest TFR (0.7), but its low fertility stems from multiple structural factors beyond education alone.

2. **Italy's TFR of 1.2** places it among the lowest in the OECD, alongside Korea, Japan, Lithuania, and Spain .

3. **The OECD average TFR is 1.46**, well below the replacement rate of 2.1, with significant convergence across countries over the past 50 years .

4. **The OECD identifies multiple pathways** linking education to fertility: opportunity costs, labour market insecurity, housing difficulties, childcare affordability, and changing attitudes toward parenthood .

5. **The "intensive parenting" norm**—rising expectations of what it means to be a "good" parent—is explicitly recognized by the OECD as a driver of fertility decline. This directly supports the decision paralysis framework developed in previous analyses of the Italian case.

6. **Italy's case differs from Korea's**: despite similar TFR levels, Italy's tertiary attainment among mothers (34.8%) is substantially lower than Korea's youth attainment (71%), suggesting that gender inequality and institutional factors play a larger role in Italy than in Korea.

7. **The education-fertility relationship is mediated** by institutional context. Mexico's TFR of 1.9, well above the OECD average, demonstrates that lower educational attainment correlates with higher fertility, though this pattern is evolving as Mexico converges toward lower fertility levels. The relationship is far from deterministic, with countries like France and Sweden showing that high education can coexist with relatively higher fertility under supportive institutions.

8. **Limitations**: Full cross-country correlation analysis requires access to the complete *Education at a Glance 2025* dataset for all 38 OECD countries. The available search results confirm the existence of these data (Tables A1.1, A1.2, etc.) but do not provide the full country-by-country breakdown. The analysis therefore relies on the OECD's own causal framework rather than a calculated correlation coefficient.
