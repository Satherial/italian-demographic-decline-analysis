# Analysis Point 8 (Data Series): Religious vs. Civil Marriages in Italy (1970–2024)
*Secularization as a Proxy for Faith Grounding in Life Choices*

---

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the long-term decline of religious marriage rites in Italy as a measurable indicator of secularization—the erosion of Catholic faith as a foundation for life choices. The data from ISTAT and other official sources reveal a profound and accelerating transformation:

1. **From near-universal religious marriage to civil majority**: Historical estimates suggest that in 1940, approximately **98.7%** of marriages were celebrated with religious rite (though this precise figure derives from historical reconstructions rather than continuous official ISTAT series). By 2024, **61.3%** of all marriages were civil ceremonies—a complete inversion of the traditional pattern .

2. **The tipping point**: Religious marriages fell below 90% around **1977** (though this specific threshold represents an interpretation of trend data rather than an officially documented milestone), below 80% in the late 1980s, below 70% in the early 2000s, and below 50% of total marriages for the first time in **2019** (civil marriages reached 52.6% in 2019, spiking to 71.1% in 2020 due to pandemic effects, stabilizing around 61% in 2024) .

3. **Accelerating decline**: The decline of religious marriages has accelerated in recent decades. From 1990 (83.2% religious) to 2024 (38.7% religious), the share of religious marriages has more than halved .

4. **The “second marriage” effect**: Among second marriages (where at least one spouse is divorced), **95.1%** are civil ceremonies in 2024 . This reflects both canonical restrictions (divorced Catholics cannot remarry in church without annulment) and cultural choice.

5. **Regional variation**: The shift to civil marriage is most advanced in Northern Italy (58.5% civil among first marriages with both Italian spouses) and least advanced in the South (26.0% civil among first marriages with both Italian spouses), reflecting persistent regional differences in religious practice .

These data serve as a powerful proxy for secularization: the choice of marriage rite reflects whether individuals anchor a foundational life decision in religious authority (Catholic Church) or civil authority (the state). The collapse of religious marriage mirrors the broader decline in mass attendance, religious identification, and Catholic practice documented in previous analyses.

## 1. Historical Trends: 1940–2024

### 1.1 The Baseline: Mid-20th Century Religious Hegemony

ISTAT data reveal the near-total dominance of Catholic marriage rites in the decades following World War II :

| Year | Religious Marriages (%) | Civil Marriages (%) |
|------|------------------------|---------------------|
| 1940 | ~98.7%* | ~1.3%* |
| 1952 | 97.6% | 2.4% |
| 1960 | ~98% | ~2% |

*Note: The 1940 figure represents a historical estimate rather than a continuous official ISTAT series.*

> “Nel 1940 il 98,7 per cento dei matrimoni avveniva in chiesa. Ancora vent'anni dopo, i matrimoni celebrati con rito religioso superavano il 98% e bisogna aspettare il 1977 perché scendano sotto il 90%.”

*Note: This quotation represents historical reconstruction; precise annual ISTAT series for 1940 are not consistently available in official archives.*

This period represents the peak of Catholic cultural hegemony in Italy. Marriage was almost exclusively a religious institution, with civil marriage reserved for specific circumstances (mixed marriages, non-Catholics, or those explicitly rejecting the Church).

### 1.2 The Turning Point: 1970s–1990s

The introduction of divorce in 1970 (Law No. 898, December 1, 1970) marked a watershed moment in Italian family law . While divorce was initially opposed by the Church and Christian Democratic parties, its eventual passage signaled the beginning of the end of Catholic monopoly over family matters.

Key milestones:

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 1970 | Divorce legalized (Law No. 898) |
| 1974 | Divorce referendum fails to repeal the law |
| 1975 | Family law reform introduces joint property regime and gender equality |
| 1977 | Religious marriages fall below 90% for the first time* |
| 1984 | Revised Concordat between Italy and the Holy See (replacing 1929 Lateran Pacts) |

*Note: This threshold represents interpretation of trend data rather than an officially documented milestone.*

The 1970–1977 period saw the first sustained decline in religious marriages, as Italians began exercising the option of civil marriage in greater numbers .

### 1.3 Accelerating Secularization: 1990–2020

The ISTAT podcast “Il matrimonio” provides a clear time series for the modern period :

| Year | Religious Marriages (%) | Civil Marriages (%) |
|------|------------------------|---------------------|
| 1990 | 83.2% | 16.8% |
| 2010 | 63.5% | 36.5% |
| 2019 | 47.4% | 52.6% |

The 1990–2019 period saw a dramatic inversion: civil marriages went from a minority to a majority of all weddings. By 2019, for the first time in Italian history, **civil marriages exceeded religious marriages** .

### 1.4 The COVID-19 Acceleration

The pandemic created a temporary but significant acceleration in the shift to civil marriage :

> “Oltre alla forte diminuzione [dei matrimoni], emerge dal Report che più dei due terzi dei matrimoni nel 2020 sono stati celebrati con rito civile… la frequenza del rito civile era già in crescita costante. Ma in effetti il dato del 2020 mostra un incremento ancora più marcato, visto che le celebrazioni con rito civile arrivano al **71,1%**.”

This spike reflected both the closure of churches during lockdowns and the increased demand for civil ceremonies that could be conducted under health protocols. By 2021, rates returned toward pre-pandemic levels, but the acceleration of the underlying trend continued .

## 2. Current Data: 2024

### 2.1 Overall Marriages

According to ISTAT data for 2024 :

| Indicator | 2024 Value | Change vs. 2023 |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Total marriages | 173,272 | -5.9% |
| Religious marriages | — | **-11.4%** |
| Civil marriages | 61.3% of total | +2.4 percentage points |

The decline in religious marriages is **nearly double** the decline in total marriages, indicating a structural shift away from Church weddings independent of the overall decline in nuptiality.

### 2.2 Breakdown by Marriage Type

The share of civil versus religious marriages varies dramatically by type of marriage :

| Marriage Type | Civil Marriages (%) | Religious Marriages (%) |
|---------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| All marriages | 61.3% | 38.7% |
| First marriages | 50.2% | 49.8% |
| Second marriages (at least one divorced spouse) | **95.1%** | 4.9% |
| Mixed marriages (Italian-foreign) | **91.8%** | 8.2% |
| Both spouses Italian (first marriages) | 43.7% | 56.3% |

**Key observation**: The 95.1% civil marriage rate among second marriages reflects both the Catholic Church’s prohibition on remarriage without annulment and a genuine cultural shift. Even among first marriages with both spouses Italian, civil marriage now represents 43.7%—a figure that would have been unimaginable a generation ago .

### 2.3 Regional Variation

Significant regional differences persist, reflecting the North-South divide in religious practice :

| Region | Civil Marriages (First Marriages, Both Italian) |
|--------|------------------------------------------------|
| Northern Italy | 58.5% |
| Central Italy | Not specified (intermediate) |
| Southern Italy | 26.0% |

The South remains the most religious region of Italy, with civil marriage still a minority practice among first marriages. However, even in the South, the trend is toward increasing secularization .

### 2.4 Age and Demographic Context

The average age at first marriage continues to rise :

| Gender | Average Age at First Marriage (2024) |
|--------|-------------------------------------|
| Men | 34.8 years |
| Women | 32.8 years |

> “Il rinvio delle prime nozze continua: l’età media è 34,8 anni per gli uomini e 32,8 per le donne, in aumento rispetto al 2023 di 0,1 anni.”

The total first marriage rate has also declined:

| Indicator | 2024 Value |
|-----------|------------|
| First marriages per 1,000 men | 372 |
| First marriages per 1,000 women | 422 |

These figures represent declines from 2019 levels (633 and 760 respectively) .

## 4. Interpretation: Religious vs. Civil Marriage as a Secularization Proxy

### 4.1 Why Marriage Rite Matters

The choice of marriage rite is a powerful indicator of secularization for several reasons:

1. **Life choice anchor**: Marriage is one of the most significant life decisions individuals make. Choosing civil marriage represents a deliberate decision to exclude religious authority from this foundational commitment.

2. **Cost and institutional constraints**: For divorced Catholics, religious marriage requires annulment—a costly and time-consuming process. Choosing civil marriage avoids this burden, but also represents acceptance that one's marriage will not be recognized by the Church.

3. **Symbolic and practical factors**: For younger generations, choosing civil marriage may represent various factors including positive secular values, rejection of perceived Church teachings, or practical considerations.

4. **Correlation with practice**: ISTAT data show that religious marriage rates correlate strongly with mass attendance and other measures of religious practice at the regional level. *However, this correlation does not imply direct causation, as marriage decisions involve multiple factors beyond religious practice.*

### 4.2 Connection to Broader Secularization

The decline of religious marriage parallels other indicators documented in previous analyses:

| Indicator | Peak/Historical Value | Current Value |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| Religious marriages | ~98.7% (1940) | 38.7% (2024) |
| Weekly mass attendance | 36.4% (2001) | 17.9% (2023) |
| Catholic identification | 87.5% (2005) | 71.1% (2024) |
| Young adults (18-34) Catholic | — | 58.3% (2024) |

The marriage data reinforce the conclusion that **Italian secularization is not merely a matter of declining attendance but a fundamental restructuring of life choices** away from religious frameworks.

### 4.3 The Generational Dimension

The near-universality of civil marriage for second marriages (95.1%) and the high rate among mixed marriages (91.8%) suggest that:

- **Younger cohorts** are increasingly likely to marry civilly regardless of marital history
- **The annulment process** is increasingly irrelevant to how Italians organize their family lives
- **Religious marriage** is becoming a minority practice concentrated among first-time, both-Italian, Southern couples

## 5. Limitations of Available Data

| Limitation | Explanation |
|------------|-------------|
| **Historical data precision** | Some historical figures (particularly pre-1970 data) represent estimates or reconstructions rather than continuous official ISTAT series. The 1940 figure of ~98.7% and the 1977 threshold of 90% should be understood as plausible interpretations rather than precisely documented official statistics. |
| **No distinction within religious rites** | ISTAT data do not distinguish between Catholic and other religious rites (Orthodox, Protestant, etc.). However, Catholic marriages historically constitute the overwhelming majority of religious weddings in Italy. |
| **No individual-level data on motives** | The data do not reveal why couples choose civil vs. religious marriage (e.g., practical constraints vs. genuine secularization). |
| **Historical data gaps** | Continuous annual data from 1970 to 2024 are not provided in the search results; the analysis relies on milestone years. |
| **Pandemic effect** | 2020–2021 data are distorted by COVID-19 restrictions; the trend line should be interpreted with this in mind. |

## 6. Conclusions

1. **The shift from religious to civil marriage in Italy represents one of the most dramatic indicators of secularization in the Western world**. From near-universal religious marriage in 1940 (approximately ~98.7% based on historical estimates), Italy has reached a point where **61.3% of marriages are civil ceremonies** .

2. **The tipping point occurred in 2019**, when civil marriages became the majority for the first time in Italian history .

3. **The decline has accelerated in recent decades**: from 1990 (83.2% religious) to 2024 (38.7% religious), the share of religious marriages has more than halved .

4. **Regional and generational divides persist**: The South remains more traditional (26% civil among first marriages with both Italian spouses) than the North (58.5% civil), and second marriages are overwhelmingly civil (95.1%) .

5. **Marriage rite choice serves as a powerful proxy for secularization**: It captures whether individuals anchor foundational life decisions in religious authority or civil authority—a choice that reflects deeper shifts in values, identity, and practice.

6. **The data support the broader thesis**: The collapse of religious marriage mirrors the decline in mass attendance, Catholic identification, and other indicators of faith grounding in Italian society. *While the choice of marriage rite correlates with secularization trends, it should be noted that this relationship reflects correlation rather than direct causation, as multiple factors influence marriage decisions including practical considerations, costs, and institutional constraints.* This erosion of traditional Catholic culture is a significant factor in Italy's demographic decline, as the pronatalist framework historically provided by the Church loses its influence over life choices.
