# Analysis Point 8 (Expanded): Decline of Catholic Identity by Generation in Italy
*Data from CENSIS/CEI 2024, European Values Study (EVS), and World Values Survey (WVS)*

---

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the decline of Catholic identity across generations in Italy using data from the **CENSIS survey commissioned by the Italian Episcopal Conference (CEI) 2024**, the **European Values Study (EVS)**, and the **World Values Survey (WVS)**. The evidence reveals a **profound generational rupture** in Italian Catholic identity:

1. **Overall Catholic identification**: According to estimates reported by journalistic sources based on CENSIS/CEI surveys, approximately 71% of Italians still identify as Catholic, but this masks dramatic generational differences. Among young adults aged 18–34, only about 58% identify as Catholic—a decline of roughly 13 percentage points from the national average .

2. **Practice rates among young Catholics**: According to the same survey estimates, only about **~11%** of young adults who identify as Catholic attend services regularly, compared to approximately **~15%** among the general Catholic population .

3. **Generational gender gap**: Academic analysis of EVS data (1980–2017) reveals that the generational divide is **more pronounced among women than men**, particularly in the behavioral (practice) and spiritual (belief) dimensions of religiosity .

4. **Longitudinal trends**: WVS 2005 data showed **87.5%** of Italians identifying as Catholic . The decline to approximately **~71%** in 2024 represents a **substantial drop of over 15 percentage points** over approximately two decades, though cross-survey comparability is limited due to methodological differences .

5. **The “internalized faith” phenomenon**: According to survey estimates, more than half (around **56%**) of Italians who have stepped away from regular practice state they live out their faith privately, reflecting a shift from institutional to personal spirituality .

These findings demonstrate that Italy is no longer an exception to European secularization trends. The generational rupture in Catholic identity—particularly among young women—likely contributes to fertility patterns alongside other structural factors, as traditional Catholic pronatalist values are failing to transmit to the next generation of mothers.

## 1. Overall Catholic Identity in Italy: CENSIS/CEI 2024 Data

### 1.1 Key Findings from the CENSIS Survey

A major study commissioned by the Italian Episcopal Conference (CEI) and conducted by CENSIS, published in November 2024, provides the most recent comprehensive data on Catholic identity in Italy .

| Indicator | Percentage |
|-----------|------------|
| Italians identifying as Catholic | **~71%** |
| Regular churchgoers (among Catholics) | **~15%** |
| Occasional participants | **~35%** |
| Non‑practicing Catholics | **~21%** |
| Private/internalized faith (among those who stepped away) | **~56%** |

### 1.2 The “Internalized Faith” Trend

The survey reveals a significant shift in how Italians relate to their faith:

> “The trend of 'internalized faith' is reshaping how Italians relate to the Church, as more than half (around 56%) who step away from regular practice state they live out their faith privately” .

This finding supports the broader secularization thesis: religious identity is becoming decoupled from institutional participation—a phenomenon sociologists have termed “believing without belonging” or, in the Italian context, “low‑intensity religion” .

### 1.3 Reasons for Disconnection

When asked why they felt disconnected from the Church, Italians cited :

| Reason | Percentage |
|--------|------------|
| Church is “too outdated” | 45.1% |
| Church lacks “clear direction” | 27.8% |
| Church is overly patriarchal (among women) | 46.5% |

The credibility crisis resulting from abuse scandals has also damaged the Church’s image: nearly **7 in 10 Italians** (including 60% of practicing Catholics) state that abuse within the Church has damaged its image .

## 2. Generational Breakdown: Youth Disaffiliation

### 2.1 Young Adults (18‑34)

The CENSIS data reveal a dramatic generational gap :

| Age Group | Identify as Catholic | Regular Churchgoers (within group) |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------------------------|
| General population | **~71%** | **~15%** (of all Catholics) |
| 18–34 year‑olds | **~58%** | **~11%** (of young Catholics) |

The gap is stark: while about 71% of all Italians identify as Catholic, only **~58% of young adults** do so—a decline of roughly 13 percentage points in a single generation .

Among young Catholics, only **~11%** attend services regularly, compared to about **~15%** among the general Catholic population .

### 2.2 The 9:1 Ratio

Statistician Roberto Volpi, analyzing ISTAT census data, provided an illustrative estimate summarizing the generational rupture :

> “At age 18‑19, for every young person who attends church regularly, nine never set foot in church.”

This represents a complete inversion of the pattern that prevailed in Italy as recently as the 1990s.

## 3. Longitudinal Trends: World Values Survey and European Values Study

### 3.1 World Values Survey 2005 (Wave 5)

The World Values Survey 2005 for Italy provides a benchmark for Catholic identification in the mid‑2000s :

| Religious Denomination | Percentage |
|------------------------|------------|
| Roman Catholic | **87.5%** |
| None | 12.0% |
| Other | 0.5% |

At that time, 87.5% of Italians identified as Catholic—substantially higher than the approximately **~71%** reported in the 2024 CENSIS survey . This represents a **substantial decline of over 15 percentage points** over approximately two decades, though cross-survey comparability should be interpreted with caution due to methodological differences between WVS and CENSIS approaches.

### 3.2 EVS Longitudinal Data

The European Values Study (EVS) has been conducted in Italy since 1981 (waves: 1981, 1990, 1999, 2008, 2017) . Academic research using pooled EVS data has documented the long‑term decline in Italian religiosity:

> “Religiosity is declining in Italy, as documented by Pisati (2000) and more recently by Vezzoni et al. (2015) showing that attendance at Mass in Italy has decreased since the 1960s, despite a period of stability in the 1980s” .

The pattern follows a lying “S” curve:
- **1960s–1970s**: Rapid decline
- **1980s–1990s**: Stabilization (the “Italian exceptionalism” period)
- **2000s–present**: Accelerating decline

## 4. Generational and Gender Analysis: EVS Longitudinal Study

### 4.1 The Academic Study: “Uno strappo al femminile?”

A peer‑reviewed academic study by an Italian research team, published in *Sociologia e ricerca sociale* (2023), specifically investigates gender differences in the generational divide in Italian religiosity using EVS data from 1980 to 2017 .

**Key research question**:
> “Gender differences in the divide between generations in the religious sphere” .

The analysis—guided by a multidimensional approach to religiosity—reveals:

> “The results reveal not only that, as expected, young people are generally more secularised than the less young; but also, that being young weakens more certain aspects of female rather than male religiosity, although women remain on average more religious than men.”

### 4.2 Key Findings

| Dimension of Religiosity | Finding |
|--------------------------|---------|
| **Spiritual dimension** (beliefs) | The generational distance among women is **greater** than among men |
| **Behavioral dimension** (practice) | The generational distance among women is **greater** than among men |
| **Identity‑cultural dimension** | Generational differences are less pronounced |
| **Institutional dimension** | Generational differences are less pronounced |

The study concludes:

> “The distance between generations of women in religious commitment is greater than it is among men with respect to the spiritual dimension (relating to beliefs) and the behavioral dimension (relating to practice) of religiosity.”

### 4.3 Implications for Fertility

This finding is crucial for understanding the connection between secularization and fertility decline. Women have historically been:
- More religious than men
- The primary agents of religious socialization for children
- More influenced by Catholic teachings on family formation, contraception, and motherhood

The fact that the generational rupture is **more pronounced among women**—and specifically in the behavioral and spiritual dimensions most directly linked to fertility decisions—suggests that the cultural transmission of pronatalist Catholic values has been fundamentally disrupted, although women remain on average more religious than men.

## 5. Educational Expansion and Declining Religiosity

### 5.1 The Dražanová Study (2022)

Lenka Dražanová’s analysis of EVS Italian data (1990–2020), published in *Quaderni di Sociologia*, examines the relationship between educational expansion, declining religiosity, and gender egalitarianism .

**Key finding**:

> “Results show that overall, the changes over time in gender egalitarianism attributable to education and religiosity are mostly driven by period effects, not cohort replacement.”

This means that the decline in religiosity affects all generations simultaneously, rather than being purely a cohort replacement phenomenon. However, the CENSIS data showing lower Catholic identification among youth suggest that period effects have had a stronger impact on younger cohorts.

### 5.2 Educational Expansion in Italy

Dražanová documents the scale of educational expansion :

> “Since the 1950s Italian education expanded rapidly. While in the 1940s the number of enrolled students in Italian universities has been below 0.5 million, it has become about 2 million in the 1990s.”

Higher education is consistently associated with:
- Lower religiosity
- More gender‑egalitarian attitudes
- Delayed family formation
- Lower fertility intentions

The simultaneous expansion of education and decline of religiosity among Italian women has created a new cultural landscape in which the traditional Catholic framework for family formation has been largely abandoned by younger generations.

## 7. Synthesis: Generational Rupture and Fertility Implications

### 7.1 The Three Dimensions of Generational Decline

The available data reveal a three‑dimensional generational rupture:

1. **Identification**: Catholic identity among 18–34 year‑olds (**~58%**) is substantially lower than the national average (**~71%**) .

2. **Practice**: Among young Catholics, regular attendance (**~11%**) is even lower than among older Catholics .

3. **Gender**: The rupture is **more pronounced among women**—and specifically in the behavioral and spiritual dimensions most directly linked to fertility decisions, although women remain on average more religious than men.

### 7.2 Mechanisms Linking Secularization to Fertility

The generational decline in Catholic identity affects fertility through several mechanisms:

| Mechanism | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Loss of pronatalist framework** | Catholic teachings encouraging large families, condemning contraception, and valorizing motherhood lose influence |
| **Reduced community support** | Parish communities that historically supported mothers shrink as practice declines |
| **Transmission failure** | Young women raised without strong religious identity do not transmit pronatalist values to their children |
| **Moral autonomy** | Fertility decisions become matters of personal preference rather than religious obligation |

### 7.3 The “Gender Factor” Revisited

The finding that the generational divide is **greater among women than men** in behavioral and spiritual dimensions is particularly significant for fertility analysis. Women are the primary decision‑makers regarding childbearing; the erosion of Catholic identity among young women means that the traditional cultural framework supporting family formation has been fundamentally weakened at the level of the very population whose reproductive choices determine fertility rates.

## 9. Conclusions

1. **Catholic identity in Italy is declining generationally**: Only **~58%** of 18–34 year‑olds identify as Catholic, compared to about **~71%** of the general population—a decline of roughly 13 percentage points .

2. **Practice rates among young Catholics are minimal**: Only **~11%** of young Catholics attend services regularly .

3. **The generational rupture is gendered**: Academic analysis of EVS data (1980–2017) reveals that the generational distance among women is **greater than among men** in the behavioral and spiritual dimensions of religiosity—precisely the dimensions most directly linked to fertility decisions, although women remain on average more religious than men.

4. **Long‑term trends show accelerating decline**: WVS data show Catholic identification fell from **87.5% in 2005 to approximately ~71% in 2024**—a substantial decline of over 15 percentage points, though cross-survey comparability should be interpreted with caution due to methodological differences .

5. **“Italian exceptionalism” has ended**: Italy no longer stands out as a European country resistant to secularization; it now follows the pattern of its northern neighbors .

6. **Educational expansion is correlated with secularization**: The rapid expansion of higher education in Italy since the 1950s has been associated with declining religiosity and more gender‑egalitarian attitudes .

7. **Implications for fertility**: The generational rupture in Catholic identity—particularly among young women—represents a fundamental shift in the cultural framework supporting family formation. Traditional Catholic pronatalist values are failing to transmit to the next generation of mothers, likely contributing to Italy’s demographic decline alongside other structural factors.
