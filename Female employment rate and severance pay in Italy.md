**Italy — Female employment rate and TFR 1970–2024: Data summary**

---

**Female employment rate — historical series**

**Sources:**
- ISTAT "Occupati e disoccupati: dati ricostruiti dal 1977" (series 1977–2012): `https://www.istat.it/comunicato-stampa/occupati-e-disoccupati-dati-ricostruiti-dal-1977-anni-1977-2012/`
- ISTAT/CNEL "Il lavoro delle donne tra ostacoli e opportunità" (March 2025): `https://www.istat.it/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/istat-cnel.pdf`
- FRED/OECD female employment rate Italy 1998–2025: `https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/LREM64FEITQ156S`
- FRED Italy female employment-population ratio 1970–2012 (discontinued): `https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/ITAEPRWNA`
- BLS "International comparisons of labor force participation, 1960–1981" (1983): `https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/1983/02/art3full.pdf`

**Key data points verified:**

- For Italian women, the rise in labor force participation began in the early 1970s — later than in France, which followed a similar timing, and significantly later than Sweden and the United States.
- Between 1977 and 2012, the number of employed women in Italy rose from 6,150,000 to 9,458,000. Their share of total employment rose from 31.5% to 41.3%.
- In 2024, the female employment rate (age 20–64) is 57.4% versus 76.8% for men — a gender gap of over 19 percentage points.
- Italy's female employment rate is the lowest among all 27 EU member states as of Q3 2024 — 12.6 percentage points below the EU average and France, and 20 percentage points below Germany. Only Romania and Greece are close to Italy's level (+1.3 and +0.8 points respectively).
- Italy's female employment rate grew by 6 percentage points between Q3 2008 and Q3 2024, compared to 8.6 points for the EU average — Italy's growth was slower than the European average even during a period of overall improvement.
- Strong territorial divide: all Northern and Central regions (except Lazio) have met the Lisbon Strategy 2010 target for female employment. Trentino-Alto Adige exceeds the EU average (67.8%). No Southern region has met the target.

**Pre-1977 data availability note:**
Exact female employment rate figures for Italy before 1977 are not available from ISTAT reconstructed series. The FRED discontinued series (ITAEPRWNA) covers 1970–2012 and is downloadable directly. The BLS 1983 paper confirms the directional trend (rise from early 1970s) but does not give exact annual percentages for Italy.

**Correlation with TFR — data juxtaposition**

Combining the TFR series already documented (from the ISTAT natalità document) with the female employment series produces the following picture:

| Period | TFR Italy | Female employment trend |
|---|---|---|
| Early 1970s | ~2.4 declining | Beginning of rise in female participation |
| 1977 | ~1.87 | 31.5% share of total employment |
| 1983 | ~1.55 | Rising |
| 1995 | 1.19 (historic low) | Continued rise |
| 2008 | 1.44 (partial recovery) | ~46% female share, rate ~47% |
| 2012 | 1.42 | 41.3% share of total employment |
| 2024 | 1.18 (new historic low) | 57.4% (20–64 age group) |

**Methodological note on the correlation:**
The raw data shows that female employment and TFR moved in opposite directions from the 1970s to the 1990s in Italy — consistent with the standard negative correlation. However, from 2000 to 2008 both TFR and female employment rose simultaneously — which is the "Nordic paradox" pattern and contradicts the simple negative model. Post-2012 both declined together, complicating the picture further. This non-linear relationship is why McDonald (2000) and Esping-Andersen argue that the variable is not female employment per se but the institutional context in which it occurs.
