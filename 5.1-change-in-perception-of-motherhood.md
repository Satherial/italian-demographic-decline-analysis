**Key measurement instruments**

The standard indicators used in the European literature to operationalize attitudes toward motherhood — confirmed textually from Genus (2023) and Lomazzi (2017):

- EVS item: **"Pre-school children suffer when mothers work"** — measures traditional view of motherhood as primary female role
- ISSP/EVS item: **"A job is alright but what most women really want is a home and children"** — measures attitudes toward women's dual role as worker and caregiver
- Agreement with both items = traditional view; disagreement = perception of motherhood as individual choice

**Trend 1988–2008 — path-dependency of traditionalism (Lomazzi 2017)**

Source: EVS 1990, 1999, 2008 — ISSP 1988, 1994 — WVS 2005 — pooled dataset
*European Societies* 19(4): 370–395

Key findings — verified from abstract and secondary literature:
- Support for gender equality in the labour market (public sphere) increased across the period for both men and women — consistent with postmaterialist theory
- Support for the dual role of women (combining work and family) declined from the late 1980s to the end of the 1990s, with only a slight increase from 2008 — a counter-movement to the public sphere trend
- "The results address a reinforcement of traditionalism mainly due to the period effect that shows regional differences given by history" — verified direct quote from abstract
- Core structural finding: Italy developed a divergence between public sphere egalitarianism (women working = acceptable) and private sphere traditionalism (women as primary caregivers = persistent) — this divergence is what the McDonald (2000) framework predicts as the cause of very low fertility
- Note: the detailed formulations about postmaterialist values are correctly attributed as paraphrases of the paper's argument, not verified direct quotes from the full text

**Trend 1990–2020 — educational expansion, secularization, and gender egalitarianism (Quaderni di Sociologia 2022)**

Source: EVS Italian sample 1990–2020
Note: findings below are paraphrases consistent with the paper's argument — not verified direct quotes from full text in this session

- Support for gender egalitarianism has been on the rise in Italy since 1990
- Changes are driven primarily by period effects, not cohort replacement — this means the shift affects all age groups simultaneously and is not a structural generational change; it is therefore potentially reversible
- Educational expansion and declining religiosity are identified as the two main drivers of the egalitarian shift

**Behavioral change 1990s–2020s — structural convergence (Aassve et al. 2024, PDR)**

Source: ISTAT Household Multipurpose Survey "Families, Social Subjects, and Life Cycle" 2016, N=32,000 — *Population and Development Review* 50(4): 1267–1288

All figures below verified directly from paper text:

- In the late 1990s, out-of-wedlock childbearing in Italy remained confined to approximately 5%; in the same period, the UK and Netherlands had surged to approximately 25%, France was just below 50%, and Norway just above 50%
- By the end of the 2020s, out-of-wedlock births in Italy reached 38% — close to the rising European average of 42%
- Out-of-wedlock childbearing has tripled since the beginning of the 21st century
- Proportion of civil marriages increased from less than 20% to 50% of all marriages in the last two decades; in the early 1970s only 2% of marriages were civil
- "Cohabitation has slowly, but steadily, become part of family life" — verified direct quote
- "New generations of parents have a more tolerant attitude towards new family life courses experienced by their offspring" — verified direct quote
- Key interpretation: behavioral change (cohabitation, out-of-wedlock birth, civil marriage) has been accelerating faster than attitudinal change as measured by EVS survey items — the two are not synchronized

**Parenthood as self-fulfillment — current attitudes among young Italians (Genus 2025)**

Source: Istituto Giuseppe Toniolo 2020 Youth Report survey — verified from ResearchGate abstract

- Four in ten childless 18–34 year-olds state they would feel fully realized even without children, irrespective of their stated fertility intentions — verified from abstract
- Economic and employment insecurity help explain this stance, but cultural factors are also independently significant — verified from abstract
- Traditional gender values correlate positively with fertility intentions, but not — among women — with the importance attached to childbearing as a source of personal fulfillment — verified from abstract
- This finding introduces a structural paradox: traditional women intend to have more children but do not see motherhood as essential to self-realization; non-traditional women perceive motherhood as a choice but are less likely to intend to have children

**Qualitative evidence — Florence study (PubMed 2020)**

Source: grounded theory qualitative study, Florence — verified from abstract

- Women's shifting roles and reduced marital salience contributed to decisions to delay or forgo childbearing — paraphrase of abstract, not direct quote
- Perceived postpartum challenges documented: employment discrimination, childrearing costs, variable paternal support — verified from abstract
- Lingering expectations that women prioritise motherhood above other pursuits were noted by participants — verified from abstract
- "Some participants discussed increased parental responsibility sharing" — verified from abstract as a directional finding, not quantified

**"Fertility Day" campaign and its cultural reception (MDPI 2025)**

Source: focus groups Southern Italy 2022–2024, MDPI Social Sciences — verified from ResearchGate abstract

- The 2016 Italian "Fertility Day" government campaign (launched under Minister Lorenzin) framed fertility as a national public health duty rather than an individual choice — confirmed historical fact
- MDPI (2025) describes the campaign as "a good example of biopower that establishes the issue of fertility in its public health dimension, as the new frontier for the control and disciplining of citizens, especially women, who are considered to be the main persons responsible for reproductive choices" — verified from abstract
- The campaign's widespread rejection by Italian public opinion is a documented cultural marker of the shift from duty-based to choice-based perception of motherhood

---

**Contraceptive attitudes as proxy for shift toward choice (PMC 2014)**

Source: "An Imperfect Contraceptive Society: Fertility and Contraception in Italy", *Population Studies* — verified from PMC abstract

- In France in 1978: 60% of women in couples using the pill or IUDs — verified
- In Italy in 1979: only 16% of women in couples using the pill or IUDs — verified
- By 1996 in Italy: 34% — verified
- Italy is characterized as an "imperfect contraceptive society" — a society achieving high fertility control through traditional methods (primarily coitus interruptus) rather than modern contraception — verified
- This structural difference indicates that the cultural shift toward motherhood as a deliberate individual choice arrived significantly later in Italy than in France or Northern Europe

---

**Gender equality attitudes and TFR — macro-level evidence (ESR 2015)**

Source: European Sociological Review, 27 countries, WVS and EVS 1990–2009 — verified from Oxford Academic abstract

- "To be positively associated with fertility, gender-equitable attitudes must not only be strongly present overall, but also similarly diffused among women and men" — verified from abstract
- Where institutions and policy context do not promote the combination of work and motherhood — as in Mediterranean countries — persistent low fertility is more likely — verified from abstract
- Attitudinal change toward motherhood as individual choice must be accompanied by institutional change to produce a fertility effect; attitudinal change alone is insufficient
