**Core theoretical framework — the male breadwinner model in Italy**

Source: Vignoli, Drefahl & De Santis (2012) — verified direct quotes:

- "Italian couples are neither fully traditional nor entirely modern: the 'first pillar' (i.e., a male partner with a stable and well-paid job) is still crucial in directing fertility decisions, because it gives the household a feeling of relative economic security"
- "This 'old' family typology is becoming rare"
- "A permanent occupation for both partners is associated with higher fertility, while alternative job typologies for either of the two depress fertility"
- Dataset: EU-SILC Italy 2004–2007, four waves

Source: Alderotti et al. (2025), European Sociological Review — verified:
- Italy retains a dominant male-breadwinner context in which occupational status represents a main determinant of fertility intentions, especially among men
- Working conditions represent a stronger predictor of fertility intentions among men than among women in the Italian context

**Male employment and fertility — key empirical findings**

**Busetta, Mendola & Vignoli (2019) — correctly attributed finding:**
- For Italy, his persistent joblessness is more significant to a couple's fertility intentions than hers — verified as correctly attributed to this paper, not to Vignoli et al. (2012)

**Alderotti et al. (2021), Demography — European meta-analysis including Italy:**
- There is consensus in the literature that high income leads to higher fertility among men; findings on women are less consistent
- College-educated and high-earning women tend to delay motherhood and remain more often childless — the reverse pattern applies less consistently to men
- Employment is positively associated with having a child for men; men's earnings or employment are positively linked to fertility even after accounting for women's characteristics
- For men, a stable and well-paid job reinforces the self-fulfilment motive to have children by confirming the traditional breadwinner role
- Oppenheimer's Uncertainty Thesis (1994): employment insecurity hampers male breadwinning capacities and thus negatively affects family formation; women may invest in care work to compensate for lacking employment success, producing theoretically ambiguous fertility effects

**Alderotti et al. (2025), European Sociological Review — cohort perspective:**
- Employment instability has been institutionally concentrated on highly specific working-age population groups — namely youth — generating rising uncertainty about future earnings and life-course opportunities
- For women, the link between labour market instability and fertility is theoretically ambiguous: women might rely on the alternative role of mother if labour market possibilities are limited, especially when they have a partner providing a family income
- For men the direction is consistently negative: employment instability reduces the probability of family formation

**Scherer & Brini (2023), European Journal of Population:**
- Individual employment instability — temporary employment or unemployment — negatively influences the likelihood of having a first and second child in Italy
- The negative association between fertility and employment instability has intensified over recent decades, especially for women
- For women: unemployment can be positively associated with motherhood by lowering the opportunity cost of childbearing; for men: unemployment is negatively associated with fertility by undermining the breadwinner role. These are opposite directional effects of the same labour market condition operating through different mechanisms by gender.
- Even in more traditional contexts like Italy, women's employment situation has become increasingly relevant, even though the presence of an employed male partner often remains paramount

**Barbieri et al. (2011), European Sociological Review — verified:**
- Today's young Italians form a "postponement generation" — verified direct quote
- "Not holding an insider position inhibits the assumption of family responsibilities for men" — verified direct quote

**Education and family formation — timing vs probability distinction**

Source: Cantalini (2017), Research in Social Stratification and Mobility — verified as main finding from academic profile and Neodemos commentary:

- Higher education delays family formation for both men and women through longer time in education and longer transition to the labour market
- The ultimate probability of family formation is lower among high-educated women but not among high-educated men — high-educated men catch up over the life course in terms of probability of marriage and family formation
- In Italy, higher parental education has become more strongly associated with postponement of family formation across younger cohorts
- Note: the specific formulations "totally catch up" and "stronger among younger cohorts" are consistent with the paper's findings as reported in secondary sources but are not verified as direct quotes from the paper text in this session

Source: Lyngstad & Jalovaara (2021), Population Studies:
- In Italy, socio-economic background differences in family formation timing have grown stronger over time compared to France and Sweden
- Note: this finding is not verified textually from the paper in this session — flagged as requiring direct access

**Perceived economic uncertainty as macro-level driver**

Source: Comolli & Vignoli (2021), European Sociological Review — verified in previous session:

- Part of the recent fertility decline since the Great Recession has been left unexplained by traditional macroeconomic measures
- Birth postponement may have accelerated in response to rising uncertainty, which fuelled negative expectations and declining levels of confidence about the future — irrespective of person-specific economic circumstances
- Since 2010, a persistent drop in fertility has been registered even in Nordic countries where the Great Recession was comparatively mild — not explained by traditional macroeconomic indicators

---

**Fertility intentions and male employment — gender-differentiated effects**

Source: Fiori, Rinesi & Graham (2020), Social Indicators Research — verified in previous session:

- Couples' working conditions and contextual labour market indicators are among the most relevant determinants of fertility intentions in Italy
- The highest level of education became more important for men's fertility intentions after the onset of the Great Recession
- Men in dual-earner couples display higher fertility intentions than those in one-earner couples — confirming that men are modifying the traditional male breadwinner model toward more egalitarian economic roles within the couple
- Marriage rather than cohabitation is more compatible with employment and economic certainties in Italy
