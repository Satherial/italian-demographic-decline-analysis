## Hedonistic Culture and the Rejection of Sacrifice (Dolce Vita vs. Parenthood)

- Measure the prioritization of leisure, sociality, and domestic comfort in Italian daily life through time-use data. According to Groupama/Doxa surveys, approximately 55% of Italians report spending between four and six hours per day at home, viewing their dwelling as a primary space for social interaction and personal activities (Groupama/Doxa, *Osservatorio ChangeLab*, 2023). ISTAT's systematic time-use studies provide comprehensive analysis of daily time allocation across work, leisure, social life, and care responsibilities, with documented gender differences in total workloads and multitasking patterns (ISTAT, *Tempi di vita: lavoro, conciliazione, parità di genere e benessere*, 2023).

- Examine consumption priorities and trade-off patterns in Italian household decisions. Survey responses indicate that approximately 42% of Italians would reduce dining out to afford home insurance, while about 30% would forego vacations for similar security purposes (Groupama/Doxa, *Osservatorio ChangeLab*, 2023). Homeownership rates in Italy are estimated around 70-75% according to ISTAT data, with survey-based reports suggesting figures up to 79% depending on methodology, while the average age of first-time homebuyers is approximately 33 years (Groupama/Doxa, *Osservatorio ChangeLab*, 2023; ISTAT housing statistics).

- Investigate generational time constraints and lifestyle priorities. Consumer research indicates that younger generations, particularly Gen Z, frequently cite time scarcity as a barrier to home cooking and prioritize personal time in their daily decisions (Euromonitor International, *Consumer Lifestyles in Italy*, 2024). Italian consumers across age groups express aspirations for simplified living and anticipate having more free time in the future (Euromonitor International, *Consumer Values and Behaviour in Italy*, 2024).

- Analyze the cultural significance of domestic space in Italian identity formation. Consumer surveys show that Italians associate their homes with leisure, sociality, and personal expression, viewing domestic spaces as multifunctional environments for family and social interactions. Survey responses indicate that approximately 46% of Italians consider their home "a guarantee for the future—an inheritance for children or capital in case of need" (Groupama/Doxa, *Osservatorio ChangeLab*, 2023). Consumer trends also show emphasis on energy efficiency, outdoor spaces, and sustainable living as aspects of domestic comfort optimization (Euromonitor International, *Consumer Lifestyles in Italy*, 2024).

- Explore the historical construction of Italian lifestyle preferences through post-WWII narratives of liberation from rural poverty and the emergence of consumer identity. Elizabeth Krause's ethnographic work documents how the shift from *cucina povera* (peasant poverty cuisine) to prosperity created new forms of class distinction through consumption, with local populations developing "antidotes to consumerism" while navigating tensions between traditional obligations and modern aspirations (Krause, Elizabeth, *A Crisis of Births: Population Politics and Family-Making in Italy*, Thomson/Wadsworth, 2005, Chapter 4: "Displaying Class, Consuming Distinction").

- Examine the cultural emphasis on *bella figura* (keeping up appearances) and aesthetic perfection as a dimension of Italian identity. John Hooper dedicates chapters to "Face values" and "Life as art," exploring how aesthetic concerns permeate Italian social life and how the pursuit of *bella figura* shapes everything from personal presentation to political choices (Hooper, John, *The Italians*, Viking, 2015, Chapters 6 and 7).

- Compare Italian patterns with East Asian contexts to clarify the distinctiveness of the Italian "dolce vita" orientation. In Japan and South Korea, rejection of parenthood is frequently linked to oppressive work cultures, hyper-competitive education systems, and extreme time demands from corporate employment. Japanese time-use data (National Institute of Population and Social Security Research, Tokyo, *Population Statistics of Japan*, 2023) and Korean social surveys (Statistics Korea, *Population and Housing Census*, 2022) reveal work-hour pressures that differ substantially from Italy's work-leisure balance priorities.

- Analyze generational differences in values and lifestyle priorities to understand how younger cohorts approach the sacrifice-parenthood calculus. Euromonitor data indicates that Gen Z primarily prioritizes time for themselves, while Gen X places the greatest emphasis on work-life balance. Baby boomers are more likely to adopt minimalist lifestyles, repair broken items rather than replace them, and commit to sustainable consumption. Millennials show greater concern for their employers' stances on social issues (Euromonitor International, *Consumer Lifestyles in Italy*, 2024).

- Quantify the opportunity cost of parenthood through analysis of how time allocation shifts between parents and non-parents. ISTAT's time-use surveys enable measurement of care work distribution, multitasking burdens, and the reduction in social and leisure time experienced by those with children (ISTAT, *Tempi di vita: lavoro, conciliazione, parità di genere e benessere*, 2023, Chapters 4 and 5).
