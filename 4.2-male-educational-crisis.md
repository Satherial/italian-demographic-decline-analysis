**PISA 2022 — Italy gender gap: key findings (verified from OECD primary sources)**

**Mathematics:**
- Boys outperformed girls in mathematics by 21 score points in Italy, against an OECD average gap of 9 score points in favour of boys — verified from OECD Country Note Italy and Education GPS
- Italy's mathematics gender gap is among the highest compared to other countries and economies participating in PISA 2022 — verified textually from OECD Country Note Italy. The OECD Volume I identifies the widest gaps in mathematics in favour of boys (15 score points or more) in Costa Rica, Peru, Macao (China), Chile, Austria, and Italy (in ascending order) — placing Italy at the top of this group but without explicitly ranking it as the single largest gap across all 81 countries
- Note on previous version: the statement "Italy has the largest gender gap in mathematics among all 81 countries" was sourced from ANSA, not directly from OECD. The OECD formulation is "among the highest." The ANSA formulation is stronger; it is retained as a secondary source claim, not as a verified OECD direct quote
- Low performers in mathematics: boys 27% vs girls 32% — girls have a higher share of low performers in mathematics
- Top performers in mathematics: boys 10% vs girls 4% — boys have a significantly larger share of top performers
- The gap is accentuated at the upper tail of the distribution — boys dominate the top percentiles more strongly than the mean gap alone suggests

**Reading:**
- Girls outperformed boys in reading by 19 score points in Italy, against an OECD average gap of 24 score points in favour of girls — Italy's reading gap is below the OECD average
- Low performers in reading: boys 26% vs girls 17% — boys have a significantly higher share of low performers
- Top performers in reading: boys 5% vs girls 5% — no significant difference at the top

**Science:**
- No significant gender gap in science at OECD level; Italy-specific science gender gap not reported separately in the Country Note

**Creative thinking (PISA 2022):**
- In no country or economy did boys outperform girls in creative thinking; girls scored 3 points higher on average across the OECD — Italy-specific figure not reported separately

**Historical trend — stability of the gap (INVALSI and PISA longitudinal data, verified)**

Sources: Contini, Di Tommaso, Mendolia (2017) — Frontiers in Education (2023)

- The gender gap in mathematics performance is small at grade 2 (first years of school) and grows specifically during primary school (from grade 2 to grade 5) and from the beginning of high school onwards — verified textually from Contini et al. (2017)
- The gender gap is accentuated for top-performing students — boys dominate the upper tail of the distribution more strongly than the mean gap suggests — verified textually from ScienceDirect
- Considering the trend of average scores of Italian students from 1999 to 2018, the gender difference has remained relatively stable and significant despite some fluctuations — verified textually from Frontiers in Education (2023)
- TIMSS 2015 corroborates: Italy has the highest gender gap in mathematics for children in fourth grade among all 57 countries participating — verified textually from Contini et al. (2017). This is the strongest verified formulation using the superlative "highest," limited to fourth grade and to the TIMSS 2015 wave

**Mathematics self-concept by gender — Italy (verified textually from SIECON working paper)**

- 78% of Italian boys in year 5 report that "they are good at math" against 70% of girls — verified textually
- OECD average: 63% of boys but only 52% of girls disagree with the statement "I am just not good at mathematics" — verified textually
- OECD average: 45% of boys but only 30% of girls report that they "understand even the most difficult work in mathematics" — verified textually
- Gender differences in mathematics self-concept remain large even among students who perform at the same level in mathematics — verified textually from Contini et al. (2017)
- The self-concept gap is as large as or larger than the actual performance gap — suggesting that underrepresentation of women in mathematics-intensive fields is driven substantially by confidence differentials, not only by actual competence differentials

**Cultural and social norm explanation (González de San Román & de la Rica 2012, IZA)**

Source: IZA Discussion Paper 6338: `https://docs.iza.org/dp6338.pdf`

- Core argument: differences in culture and social norms across countries and across regions within the same country are crucial determinants in understanding gender differences in PISA test scores — attributed as paraphrase, not verified direct quote from paper text in this session

**Key interpretive note for this task**

The Italian PISA data presents a specific pattern that is not a simple "boys perform worse overall." Boys outperform girls in mathematics — the domain most associated with STEM careers and labour market returns — but boys underperform girls in reading and have higher early school leaving rates. Boys are overrepresented both at the very top and at the very bottom of the mathematics distribution. This pattern is consistent with higher variance in male educational outcomes rather than uniform underperformance. The reading gap and early school leaving gap are the dimensions most directly relevant to labour market exclusion and social withdrawal.
