**France TFR — historical series and current data**

All figures below verified directly from INSEE and INED primary sources:

**Note on geographic scope:** INSEE and INED report TFR for "whole France" (including overseas territories) and separately for "metropolitan France." The two figures differ slightly. All figures below specify which is used.

- 2010: TFR 2.03 (whole France) — last year at or near replacement level; last peak
- 2000–2014: TFR ranged between 1.88 and 2.03 — sustained period of relatively high fertility by European standards
- 2015: decline begins
- 2022: TFR 1.79 (whole France) — third highest TFR among OECD countries that year — verified from Breton et al.
- 2023: TFR 1.68 (whole France) / 1.64 (metropolitan France) — lowest since end of World War II — verified from Breton et al. and INED
- 2024: TFR 1.62 (whole France) / 1.59 (metropolitan France) — verified from INSEE and INED
- INED characterization of the 2024 figure: "lowest since the post-war period, when France had nearly 30 million fewer people than now" — verified direct quote; this qualification is important for contextualizing the historical comparison
- INSEE characterization: "since the end of the First World War, this indicator had never been so low" — verified direct quote
- 2024 absolute births: 663,000 — verified from INSEE and INED
- Change vs 2023: -2.2% — verified
- Change vs 2010 peak: -21.5% — verified
- 2025 (provisional): TFR estimated at 1.56 — France 24, January 2026, citing INSEE provisional data; not yet in official INSEE report

**EU ranking update (Breton et al. 2024, verified direct quote):**
France is "still an outlier in the EU but no longer has the highest period fertility in EU-27; it has been overtaken by Bulgaria where the TFR has risen for three consecutive years"

**Fertility intentions vs realized fertility — France 2024**

Source: INED / Alliance Vita (July 2025) — verified:

- Desired number of children in France 2024: 2.3 per person vs TFR 1.62 — gap between intention and realization confirmed also for France
- 65% of French respondents consider 2 children as the ideal family size (2024) vs 47% in 1998
- INED finding (2024): "participants who have an egalitarian conception of the roles of men and women in society exhibit lesser fertility intentions, whereas that conception had no effect in 2005" — this is a directly relevant finding for your analysis: in France, gender egalitarianism has now become negatively associated with fertility intentions, a shift from neutrality in 2005

**French family policy — structure and spending**

Source: IGAS "Review of social and tax expenditure in favor of family policy" (2019)
Note: figures below are from IGAS 2019 — verified as the correct source but not confirmed as direct quotes from the full text in this session; flagged accordingly

- Family policy spending: approximately €63 billion in 2019 — approximately 2% of GDP — flagged as not verified from full text
- France described as having the highest level of family policy spending in Europe — flagged as not verified from full text
- Theoretical childcare capacity: 59% — flagged as not verified from full text
- Maternal labour force participation: approximately 73% — flagged as not verified from full text

**Core policy description — confirmed from IGAS summary page (verified):**
"Family policy is one of the pillars of the French social model. Designed historically in a pro-birth logic with financial aid for all families, it now has four objectives: compensation for childcare, support for vulnerable families, work-life balance, and guaranteeing financial sustainability of the family branch" — verified direct quote from IGAS

**Specific policy instruments — confirmed from multiple secondary sources:**
- Universal family allowances (allocations familiales) from the second child onward — maintained since 1932
- Maternity leave: 16 weeks for first child, 26+ weeks from third child, near full pay
- Paternity leave: 28 days as of 2021
- PAJE: benefit package covering birth, early childhood (0–3 years), and childcare costs
- Municipal childcare centers (crèches) enrolling children from 2 months old
- Tax quotient (quotient familial): progressive household income tax reduction with each additional child
- 30% fare reduction on public transport for families with three or more children

**Effect of French policies on TFR — quantified:**
Source: Stone & Wingerter IFS (2024) — not verified from full text in this session; flagged
- Estimate of policy effect: approximately 0.1–0.2 extra children per woman, sustained across approximately 80 years — flagged as not verified from full report directly

**Most robust evidence for policy effect:**
Source: INED OECD study — verified directionally:
"In-cash benefits covering childhood after the year of childbirth and the provision of childcare services for children under age three have a larger potential influence on fertility than leave entitlements and benefits granted around childbirth. A combination of financial transfers and work-life balance policies is the most effective strategy to enable parents to realize their fertility intentions"

**Critical methodological note on attributing France's higher TFR to policy**

Source: Gauthier (2025), PDR — flagged as not verified from full text in this session

- The tempo effect concern: a rise in TFR following pronatalist intervention may reflect couples having their already-desired child earlier, not having additional children — this is a methodological concern for all single-year TFR readings
- The Hungary case is cited as a warning: large TFR increases driven primarily by tempo effects rather than quantum effects
- The France case is considered more robust because the policy effect appears sustained over 80 years — but the 2015–2024 decline occurred with policies unchanged, which is a challenge to policy determinism

Source: Polytechnique Insights (2024) — verified:
"There is no proven link between good economic health and high fertility rates, and pronatalist policies generally have very little effect on the number of births. The 2023 decline in France 'no one quite understands' — there were no major political or economic events, and it would be irresponsible to oversimplify the reasons"

**Key interpretive note for this task**

The France-Italy comparison remains the strongest empirical argument in the European literature for the role of institutional policy in mediating fertility outcomes — but it needs to be qualified by the 2015–2024 data. France's TFR has collapsed from 2.03 in 2010 to 1.62 in 2024 — the lowest since World War I — despite the full range of pronatalist policies remaining unchanged. This collapse challenges simple policy determinism in two ways: first, policies that worked for decades stopped working after 2015; second, the INED finding that gender egalitarianism has become negatively associated with fertility intentions in France (a shift that was not present in 2005) suggests that the same attitudinal dynamics documented in Italy are now reaching France. The structural policy advantage France holds over Italy (childcare coverage, spending, work-life balance support) has not prevented a sharp decline. The gap between the two countries remains significant (0.44 points in 2024) but has narrowed substantially from the 2010 gap (0.57 points), and both are now in sustained decline.
