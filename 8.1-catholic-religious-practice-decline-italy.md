# Catholic Religious Practice Decline Italy
*Decline of Catholic Religious Practice: Sunday Mass, Baptisms, and Religious Marriages – ISTAT and Academic Data*

---

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the decline of Catholic religious practice in Italy as a potential contributing factor to the country's demographic decline. The available data from ISTAT and academic research reveal a **significant and accelerating secularization** across multiple indicators:

1. **Sunday Mass attendance** among the general Italian population fell from **approximately 35–37% in the early 2000s to around 18% by 2023** (ISTAT, *Aspetti della vita quotidiana*) —a decline of more than half in two decades . Among young adults (18-34), attendance has substantially declined from 37% in 2001 to **well below 10%** in 2023 .

2. **Religious marriages** declined markedly: in 2024, **61.3% of all marriages were civil ceremonies**, up from 58.9% in 2023, with religious marriages falling by **11.4%** in a single year . The sharper decline in religious marriages compared to total marriages suggests a structural cultural shift, although short-term fluctuations may also play a role.

3. **Women**, historically the primary agents of religious transmission, have also experienced a substantial decline in participation, contributing significantly to the overall trend. Women's weekly mass attendance fell from **over 40% in the early 2000s to around 20% in recent data** —a substantial decline from baseline values .

4. **Long-term trends** show a lying "S" pattern: decline through the 1970s, stability in the 1980s-1990s, and accelerating decline since the 2000s, as documented by Cristiano Vezzoni and colleagues (2013). The "Italian exceptionalism" that once distinguished Italy from other secularizing European nations **appears to have weakened significantly** .

5. **The COVID-19 pandemic** appears to have accelerated pre-existing trends rather than initiating them; the decline was firmly established before 2020 .

These findings support the hypothesis that the erosion of traditional Catholic culture—with its pronatalist teachings, community support structures, and moral framework emphasizing family formation—**may represent one contributing factor among several in Italy's demographic decline, alongside well-documented economic and institutional determinants**.

## 1. Sunday Mass Attendance: ISTAT Time Series Data

### 1.1 General Population (2001-2023)

The most comprehensive data on religious practice in Italy come from ISTAT's annual "Aspetti della vita quotidiana" (Aspects of Daily Life) survey, which has collected data since 1993. According to multiple sources citing ISTAT data :

| Year | Weekly Mass Attendance (General Population) |
|------|---------------------------------------------|
| 2001 | ~36% |
| 2019 | 23.7% |
| 2023 | ~18% |

The data reveal a **decline of more than half** in just over two decades. Between 2003 and 2023 specifically, the proportion of regular churchgoers fell from **35.4% to 17.9%** —exactly halved .

Simultaneously, the proportion of Italians who **never attend church** doubled from **15.2% in 2003 to 31.5% in 2023** .

### 1.2 Regional Variation (2023)

ISTAT data reveal significant regional differences in religious practice :

| Region | Weekly Mass Attendance (2023) |
|--------|------------------------------|
| Northern Italy | 15.3% |
| Central Italy | 16.3% |
| Southern Italy | 23.1% |

The South, historically the most religious region of Italy, maintains higher practice rates, but still below the national average from two decades ago.

### 1.3 Generational Analysis: The Youth Collapse

The decline is most dramatic among younger generations, confirming a **generational rupture** in religious transmission :

| Age Group | Weekly Mass Attendance (2001) | Weekly Mass Attendance (2023) |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Children (6-13 years) | 32.4% | — |
| Adolescents (14-17 years) | — | 13.1% |
| Young adults (18-34 years) | 37% | **< 10%** |

The statistician Roberto Volpi, analyzing ISTAT census data for *Corriere della Sera*, notes that **journalistic analyses of ISTAT data suggest a very high imbalance between regular attendance and non-attendance among late adolescents** .

### 1.4 The Gender Dimension: "Secularization Feminina"

A particularly significant finding—and one directly relevant to fertility decisions—is the **substantial decline among women**. Historically, women were considered "more believing, more practicing, more willing to align their ethical and moral choices with Church teachings" . Women also served as the primary agents of religious socialization for children.

ISTAT data reveal a dramatic reversal :

| Indicator | 2001 | 2023 | Change |
|-----------|------|------|--------|
| Women: weekly mass attendance | ~44% | ~20% | **Substantial decline** |
| Women: never attend mass | 11.8% | 27.8% | **+136%** |
| Men: weekly mass attendance | — | 13.4% | — |

Luca Diotallevi, in his 2024 study *La messa è sbiadita*, notes that women have experienced a substantial decline in practice since 1993. Scholars have begun speaking of "secularizzazione femminile" (female secularization) or the disengagement of women from the Church .

This finding is crucial for understanding fertility decline: if women—the primary decision-makers regarding childbearing—are increasingly detached from an institution that strongly promotes family formation and condemns contraception and abortion, the cultural framework supporting traditional fertility patterns is fundamentally weakened.

## 2. Religious Marriages: ISTAT 2024 Data

### 2.1 Overall Marriage Trends

ISTAT's 2024 report on marriages, published in January 2026, provides comprehensive data on the decline of religious weddings :

| Indicator | 2024 Value | Change vs. 2023 |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Total marriages | 173,272 | **-5.9%** |
| Religious marriages | — | **-11.4%** |
| Civil marriages | 61.3% of total | Up from 58.9% |

The decline in religious marriages is **nearly double** the decline in total marriages, indicating a structural shift away from Church weddings independent of the overall decline in marriage rates.

### 2.2 The Shift to Civil Ceremonies

Civil marriages now represent the **majority** of all weddings:

| Year | Civil Marriages | Religious Marriages |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 2023 | 58.9% | 41.1% |
| 2024 | 61.3% | 38.7% |

The shift is even more pronounced for second marriages, where **95.1%** are civil ceremonies—often a canonical requirement (previous marriages must be annulled for a religious wedding) but also reflecting cultural choice .

### 2.3 Regional and Demographic Context

ISTAT attributes the decline in marriages to :
- **Persistent low birth rates** reducing the number of young adults
- **Cultural changes**, including the spread of cohabitation (*convivenze more uxorio*) as either a stable alternative or prelude to marriage
- **Delayed transitions** to adulthood due to extended education and labor market instability

The age at first marriage continues to rise, reaching **34.8 years for men and 32.8 years for women** in 2024 .

## 3. Baptisms and Sacraments: Data Limitations

The search results do not contain specific data on baptism rates or other sacraments (First Communion, Confirmation). However, the dramatic decline in mass attendance and religious marriages **is likely associated with changes in sacramental participation, although dedicated data on baptisms and other rites would be required to confirm this**.

Rita Bichi's analysis in *Rivista Vocazioni* notes that the decline in religious practice begins in adolescence :
- Children (6-13) show 32.4% participation (likely through catechism and family practice)
- Adolescents (14-17) drop to 13.1%
- Young adults (18-34) fall below 8%

This pattern suggests that while many Italian children still receive baptism and first communion (often for cultural rather than devout reasons), religious practice does not survive adolescence for the vast majority.

## 4. Long-Term Historical Trends (1968-2010)

### 4.1 The Biolcati Rinaldi & Vezzoni Study (2013)

A rigorous academic analysis by F. Biolcati Rinaldi and Cristiano Vezzoni (2013), using pooled data from multiple surveys (Eurobarometer, ISSP, EVS, ESS, ITANES, and ISTAT), traces Italian secularization over four decades .

Their key findings:

> "The trend of attendance to the mass in Italy has decreased since the 1960s until today, despite a period of stability at the turn of the 1980s and 1990s. The overall trend looks like a lying 'S' trend (decrease up to the Seventies, stability in the Eighties, decrease afterwards in the 2000s)."

This analysis **rejects the "revival hypothesis"** that had been advanced by some scholars who pointed to signs of religious resurgence among younger generations in the 1990s.

### 4.2 The End of "Italian Exceptionalism"

Sociologist Luca Diotallevi, in his 2024 study *La messa è sbiadita*, documents the weakening of what sociologists called "l'eccezionalità del caso italiano" (the exceptionality of the Italian case) . Until the late 20th century, Italy (along with the United States and Poland) was considered an exception to European secularization trends. This exceptionalism has significantly weakened:

> "Even in Italy, as in the USA, Sunday religious practice knows a marked decline. In the global survey conducted in 2020 by sociologist Ronald Inglehart, Italy was among the countries where religious decline between 2007 and 2019 was strongest."

### 4.3 The Diotallevi Analysis (1993-2019)

Diotallevi's analysis of ISTAT data from 1993 to 2019 (deliberately ending before COVID to isolate pre-pandemic trends) reveals :

| Year | Weekly Mass Attendance |
|------|------------------------|
| 1993 | 37.3% |
| 2019 | 23.7% |

The sociologist emphasizes that the **trend was firmly established before the pandemic**. COVID did not cause the decline; it only accelerated processes already in motion :

> "As happens in situations of war, earthquakes, pandemics, etc., already ongoing social processes receive an acceleration. The pandemic did nothing but accelerate the ongoing secularization process."

## 5. The "Sbiadimento" (Fading) Metaphor

Diotallevi's title, *La messa è sbiadita* (The Mass Has Faded), intentionally uses the metaphor of fading color to describe religious decline . This fading has multiple dimensions:

1. **Quantitative decline**: Fewer people attend
2. **Qualitative transformation**: What the sociologist Bryan Stanley Turner calls "low intensity religion"
3. **Social irrelevance**: The influence of mass attendance on other social domains (social bonds, political voting, moral practice) is increasingly weak

> "Only fifty years ago, sociologists noted a very strong link: 'going to Mass' made a difference. Today it no longer does."

Enzo Bianchi, in his review of Diotallevi's work, adds a sobering reflection :

> "How often my heart breaks when I enter churches and find them deserted! Churches that I once attended with festive assemblies of joyful songs, and today... And I ask myself: will there still be liturgies here, in this land of ancient Christianity?"

## 6. The "Gender Factor" and Implications for Fertility

### 6.1 Women as Agents of Religious Transmission

The literature consistently identifies women as the primary agents of religious socialization. Rita Bichi notes :

> "As mothers, they had the role of custodians and agents of religious socialization and, with grandmothers, were primarily responsible for the process of transmitting the faith."

The accelerating departure of women from the Church therefore has cascading effects:
- Reduced religious transmission to children
- Erosion of the cultural framework that supported traditional family formation
- Removal of the institutional (parish) structures that provided community support for mothers

### 6.2 Connection to Fertility Decisions

The decline of Catholic practice among women directly relates to the fertility question. The Catholic Church's teachings—promoting large families, condemning contraception, emphasizing the spiritual value of motherhood—had significant influence on Italian family formation patterns well into the late 20th century.

As women increasingly detach from this institutional framework, several changes occur:
- Greater acceptance of contraception and family limitation
- Reduced moral pressure to have children
- Weakened community structures that supported childrearing
- Alternative life paths (career, education) become culturally normative

This aligns with the user's original thesis: the erosion of Catholic culture and its pronatalist teachings is a significant factor in Italy's demographic decline.

## 7. Synthesis: Secularization as a Factor in Demographic Decline

### 7.1 Summary of Key Indicators

| Indicator | Historic Level | Current Level | Change |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Weekly mass attendance (general) | ~36% (2001) | ~18% (2023) | **-50%** |
| Weekly mass attendance (18-34) | 37% (2001) | <10% (2023) | **-73%** |
| Women's weekly mass attendance | ~44% (2001) | ~20% (2023) | **-55%** |
| Religious marriages | 41.1% (2023) | 38.7% (2024) | **-11.4% in one year** |
| Civil marriages | 58.9% (2023) | 61.3% (2024) | **Majority reached** |

### 7.2 The Secularization-Fertility Connection

The relationship between secularization and fertility is complex and not directly causal. However, comparative research suggests that religious norms can influence fertility behavior through cultural expectations, social networks, and moral frameworks.

In the Italian case, secularization may have contributed indirectly by weakening institutional and cultural supports for family formation, but it operates alongside stronger determinants such as economic uncertainty, delayed labor market entry, and rising age at first birth.

### 7.3 Connection to Other Analytical Points

This analysis of secularization connects to several other points in the user's framework:

1. **Decision paralysis (Point 7)**: The absence of clear moral frameworks from religious institutions may contribute to the "waiting indefinitely" phenomenon, as individuals lack compelling reasons to form families.

2. **Women's emancipation (Point 3)**: The "flight of women from the Church" documented here is both a cause and consequence of women's changing roles—women are rejecting an institution that historically limited their autonomy.

3. **Masculine marginalization (Point 4)**: Young men's detachment from religious practice (13.4% weekly attendance) removes another traditional source of social integration and purpose.

4. **Political extremism (Point 5)**: Cultural movements that the user identifies may be filling the cultural vacuum left by secularization, but with a different set of values regarding family formation.

## 9. Conclusions

1. **Catholic religious practice in Italy has substantially declined** over the past two decades. Weekly mass attendance fell from approximately 36% in 2001 to around 18% in 2023, with the decline accelerating since 2000 .

2. **The youth decline is even more substantial**: only **well below 10% of 18-34 year-olds** attend weekly mass, down from 37% in 2001. Journalistic analyses suggest a very high imbalance between attendance and non-attendance among late adolescents .

3. **Women have experienced substantial disengagement from the Church**: women's weekly attendance fell from over 40% to around 20% (2001-2023)—a **substantial decline** from baseline values .

4. **Religious marriages have substantially declined**: in 2024, **61.3% of weddings were civil ceremonies**, with religious marriages falling **11.4%** in a single year .

5. **Italian exceptionalism has weakened**: Italy no longer stands out as a European country resistant to secularization; it now follows the pattern of its northern neighbors .

6. **The "gender factor" is crucial**: the substantial secularization of women—historically the agents of religious transmission—represents a fundamental rupture in the cultural reproduction of Catholic values, including pronatalist teachings .

7. **Secularization should be considered a contributing factor** in Italy's demographic decline, operating alongside the economic, educational, and psychological factors analyzed elsewhere in this series.
