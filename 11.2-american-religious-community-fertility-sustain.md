## The American Exception: Risk Tolerance, Individualism, and Religious Community

- Document the fertility gap between religiously affiliated and unaffiliated Americans using the 2024 Pew Research Center Religious Landscape Study. Among U.S. adults ages 40 to 59—those who have largely completed childbearing—Christians report having 2.2 children on average, compared to 1.8 children for the religiously unaffiliated . This gap persists even as overall fertility rates have declined: the U.S. fertility rate fell to an all-time low of 1.6 births per woman in 2024 .

- Analyze the transmission of family values through religious upbringing as a key mechanism sustaining fertility. Research from the Survey Center on American Life reveals that young adults who attend religious services weekly are significantly more likely to report that their parents emphasized the importance of marriage and family formation. Among weekly attendees, 59% say their parents discussed the importance of finding a partner or getting married, compared to 38% of those who seldom or never attend services . Similarly, 49% of weekly attendees heard from parents about the benefits of having children, versus only 29% of non-attendees . The effect is substantial: roughly two-thirds (66%) of Americans whose parents often discussed the benefits of having children report wanting children themselves, compared to only 34% of those whose parents never discussed these benefits .

- Examine the aspirational gap between religious and secular young adults regarding parenthood. Religious Americans under age 40 without children are nearly twice as likely to say they want to have children someday as their nonreligious peers—62% versus 32% . Notably, the gender gap in parenthood desire emerges only among religious Americans: 66% of religious men under 40 want children compared to 57% of religious women, while secular men and women show nearly identical preferences (31% and 33% respectively) .

- Establish the socioeconomic invariance of religious benefits on child outcomes through Robert Putnam's research in *Our Kids: The American Dream in Crisis*. Putnam documents that churchgoing children "are less prone to substance abuse, risky behavior, and delinquency" and that "compared to their unchurched peers, youth who are involved in a religious organization take tougher courses, get higher grades and test scores, and are less likely to drop out of high school" . They also "have better relations with their parents and other adults, have more friendships with high-performing peers, are more involved in sports and other extracurricular activities" . Critically, Putnam finds that this is true regardless of socioeconomic status: a child whose parents attend church regularly is 40 to 50 percent more likely to go on to college than a matched child of non-attenders, even when controlling for income and education .

- Analyze the class gap in religious participation that undermines the equalizing potential of religious communities. According to Putnam's research, religious communities in America are "important service providers for young people and the poor," with weekly churchgoers two to three times more likely to volunteer to help the poor and young people than non-churchgoers . Yet religious participation is falling much more rapidly among those without a college degree, creating a class gap that did not exist fifty years ago . This means that the communities that would most benefit from the social capital and support networks of religious affiliation are precisely those with declining access to them .

- Contextualize the American religious fertility advantage within broader secularization trends. Over the last 20 years, the percentage of Americans attending worship services weekly fell from 42% to 30% . The generational gap in religious exposure is stark: 68% of Americans now in their mid-70s or older attended services weekly during childhood, compared to less than half (48%) of those currently in their early 20s . The Survey Center on American Life notes that "declining fertility rates are probably not attributable to a single cause, but in the United States, there is a strong connection to one of the defining cultural trends of the 21st century—the collapse of religious participation" .

- Address the limitations of the religious explanation while affirming its relevance. While countries with more religious populations tend to have higher birth rates, the Survey Center on American Life cautions that "in many of the most religious countries, women have limited access to birth control and economic freedom. At this point, religion alone does not seem like an especially effective remedy to the country's plunging birthrates" . Additionally, the hesitancy among nonreligious singles to start families may stem less from lack of faith and more from "a pervasive sense of cynicism and mistrust," with young nonreligious Americans reporting higher rates of anxiety, greater pessimism about politics, and more worry about the future . Yet the same research finds that "singles without children who reported being very or completely happy with their lives were much more likely to want children than those who were unhappy (59% vs. 32%)" , suggesting that the hopefulness cultivated in religious communities may be a critical mediating factor.

- Identify the broader implications of religious community for fertility beyond individual belief. As one analysis concludes: "A society that does a better job of instilling young people with a sense of hopefulness about the future will better enable them to make forward-looking decisions. Bringing children into the world is a radical act of hope. It's a bet on the future and on ourselves. It's also a collective enterprise" . Churches and places of worship "can help foster a culture that values children and families, but they can't do it alone" .
