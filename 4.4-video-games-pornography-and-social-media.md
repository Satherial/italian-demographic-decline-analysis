**Zimbardo & Coulombe (2015) — framework and methodological status**

Source: Zimbardo, P. & Coulombe, N.D. (2015), *Man (Dis)connected: How Technology Has Sabotaged What It Means to Be Male*, Rider/Ebury Press (UK), ISBN 9781846044847. US edition: *Man, Interrupted* (2016), Conari Press, ISBN 9781573246897.
Slate critical review (May 2015): `https://slate.com/technology/2015/05/philip-zimbardos-man-disconnected-claims-porn-internet-are-destroying-men.html`
TED Blog update: `https://blog.ted.com/the-demise-of-guys-zimbardo-and-coulombe-respond-to-their-critics/`

**Verified book details:**
- Based on Zimbardo's 2011 TED talk "The Demise of Guys?" — viewed over 1.8 million times
- Core argument: young men are failing academically, socially, and sexually — described as "hooked on a cocktail of porn, video games, and prescription drugs" — paraphrase of the book's thesis, not a direct verified quote
- The book argues that readily available pornography and video games provide digital alternatives that are less demanding than real-world social interaction, with consequences for cognitive and social development — paraphrase of the book's thesis, verified as accurate from multiple secondary sources

**Verified methodological problems (from Slate 2015 and TED Blog):**
- The research base is not a peer-reviewed study but a self-selected online survey posted alongside the 2011 TED talk
- The survey collected approximately 20,000 responses
- Only 39.8% of respondents were under age 25
- Approximately one quarter of respondents were female
- The definition of "excessive" consumption was changed between the TED talk and the book: originally "2 or more hours per day" was changed to "4 or more hours per week of gaming or 2 or more hours per week of porn use" — a radical definitional shift not explained in the book
- TED Blog editorial note: "the evidence that the Internet is leading to the demise of guys is currently inconclusive" — verified direct quote from TED Blog

**Conclusion on Zimbardo for this research:**
The book functions as a hypothesis generator and conceptual framework, not as an empirical data source. It should be cited as such. All empirical claims about Italian young men require Italy-specific primary data, documented below.

**Video game use in Italy — verified data**

Primary source: IIDEA (Italian Interactive Digital Entertainment Association), "Essential Facts About Video Games in Italy 2024" (PDF, April 2025): `https://iideassociation.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/EN-IIDEA_2024-Essential-Facts-About-Video-Games-in-Italy.pdf`

**All figures below verified directly from IIDEA 2024 PDF:**

- Total video gamers in Italy 2024: approximately 14 million — increase of 8% compared to 2023
- Gender distribution: 59% male (approximately 8.2 million); 41% female (approximately 5.7 million)
- Growth rate by gender: female gamers +14.4% year-on-year; male gamers +3% year-on-year (note: the English-language version of the IIDEA press release rounds to +2.5% for males — minor discrepancy, likely rounding)
- 68% of gamers play at least once per week
- Average weekly gaming time Q4 2024: 7.5 hours per week per gamer
- Age distribution: 84% adults aged 18–64; 16% children and adolescents aged 6–17
- A quarter of Italian gamers are aged 15–24; another quarter are aged 45–64
- Gaming platforms: 74.1% mobile; 44.2% console; 34.4% PC
- Total Italian gaming market revenue 2024: €2.367 billion (+3% vs 2023)
- Italy is the fifth largest gaming market in the EMEA region (behind UK, France, Germany, Spain)
- Methodological note: from 2024, IIDEA included non-binary individuals in the sample for the first time — approximately 100,000 respondents

**Data flagged as unverified in this session:**
- "60% of hardcore gamers in Italy are men; male hardcore gamers aged 18–24 made up 17% of all high-frequency players" (Osservatorio Italiano Esports 2021) — not confirmed textually in this session; flagged
- "36% of young Italian video game players spent between one and three hours gaming daily" (Skuola Network / Statista 2021) — not confirmed textually in this session; flagged

**Pornography use in Italy — data availability and gaps**

**Global reference data (not Italy-specific — verified from AddictionHelp.com citing peer-reviewed literature):**
Source: `https://www.addictionhelp.com/porn/statistics/`

- For men aged 18–26: 45% had viewed pornography in the 1973–1980 cohort; increasing to 61% in the 1999–2012 cohort — global figures
- Men are approximately 4 times more likely to watch pornography than women: 44% of men vs 11% of women report viewing pornography in the past month — global figures
- These are global averages, not Italy-specific data

**Italy-specific pornography data — acknowledged gap:**
- No Italian primary source equivalent to a national survey on pornography use frequency disaggregated by gender and age was found in this session
- Pornhub Italy-specific data (gender breakdown and age distribution) exists on Statista as premium statistics — not accessible without subscription
- The ISS and Eurispes have published aggregate data on digital addictions (documented in the previous task) but not a standalone pornography use survey for Italy disaggregated by gender
- The most relevant Italy-specific proxy available is the ESPAD Italia 2024 data on digital behavior among adolescents (documented in the previous task) — but this covers gambling and gaming more than pornography specifically

**Sexual behavior of young Italians — relevant proxy (Genus 2020)**

Source: "Catching up! The sexual behaviour and opinions of Italian students (2000–2017)", *Genus* (2020): `https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s41118-020-00085-4`

- Paper exists and is published open access in Genus — verified from Springer
- Core finding (verified as the paper's main argument from abstract and secondary citations): a convergence in sexual behavior and opinions between Italian young men and women between 2000 and 2017 was documented — men's behavior became more similar to women's in stable relationship contexts (first intercourse with a partner of roughly the same age, in a stable relationship, with less infidelity)
- Note: the specific formulations about "feminisation of male sexual opinions" and "masculinisation of female opinions" are paraphrases consistent with the paper's findings — not verified as direct quotes from the full text in this session

**Social media use among young Italian men — verified data**

Source: Eurispes Rapporto Italia 2025 (verified in previous task): `https://eurispes.eu/ricerca-rapporto/rapporto-italia-2025/`

Among 18–24 year-olds (all genders — gender disaggregation not available in public extracts):
- 78.1% use social media habitually
- 68.5% feel the need to connect frequently
- 53.3% feel more free to express themselves through social networks
- 50.9% feel less alone thanks to digital use
- 49.7% perceive a loss of time linked to social media use
- 43.0% report a sense of estrangement from the real world

**Gender disaggregation — acknowledged gap:**
AGCOM does not systematically publish gender-disaggregated data on social media hours. We Are Social / Datareportal Italy 2025 provides platform-level gender breakdown (Pinterest 70.6% female; gaming-related platforms predominantly male) but not hours-of-use data by gender. No Italy-specific peer-reviewed study on social media hours disaggregated by gender among young men was found in this session.
