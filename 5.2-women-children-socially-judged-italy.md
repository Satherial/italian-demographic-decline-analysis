**Italy — Women who want children but feel socially judged: Data summary**

---

**Key finding upfront — important clarification on the task**

A direct survey measuring specifically "Italian women who want children but feel socially judged for that choice" does not exist as a standalone instrument. What the literature documents instead are two related but distinct phenomena: (1) women who feel judged and penalized for *having* children (workplace discrimination, maternal guilt, the motherhood penalty); and (2) the gap between personal ideals of motherhood and perceived societal expectations. Both are documented empirically and are relevant to this task.

---

**MatGuilt Project — Italy 2024 (most directly relevant study found)**

Source: Matguilt online survey, N=4,500 Italian women aged 20–50, 2024. Principal investigators: Kulic, Dotti Sani, Jeannet, Stoicescu (University of Milan, PRIN 2022 grant). Published results: `https://www.spstrend.it/ideal-mother-italy-personal-preferences-social-expectations/` — Project page: `https://www.spstrend.it/matguilt/`

Italian women ideally wish to balance work and motherhood. As Italy recorded the lowest maternal employment rate in the European Union — 61.2% (Eurostat 2024) — this striking disparity between the reality of low employment and the ideal of maternal employment is worth further investigation.

Women are more likely to align with extensive motherhood practices in their own lives yet believe that Italian society still values an intensive model centered on full-time caregiving and maternal self-sacrifice.

Italian women's personal ideals still reflect the extensive motherhood model — combining work and motherhood — more than the intensive one. The only characteristic of intensive motherhood that women favor is overseeing childcare, suggesting some traditional elements remain. Preferences for extensive motherhood are especially strong among tertiary-educated women and those living in less traditional regions.

Regional differences are most pronounced regarding employment: women in Northern and Central regions are significantly more likely to view working mothers — both full-time and part-time — as ideal, compared to women in Southern regions. Regarding perceptions of societal ideals, the regional gap is similar and, if anything, less pronounced.

**MatGuilt project definition and scope** (verified from project page):
Maternal guilt is defined as mothers' feelings of inability to live up to contemporary cultural ideals of the "good mother", which set overly high standards in terms of maternal physical, psychological, and intellectual dedication toward children. The impossibility of conforming to the "myth of motherhood" results in the feeling of maternal guilt. The premise of the MatGuilt project is that debunking motherhood ideals may be a critical step toward higher levels of female employment and higher fertility.

**"Intensive mothering" ideology and social judgment in Italy**

Source: Journal of Romance Studies (2015), qualitative study of 21 highly educated women in Turin, 2010–2012: `https://www.liverpooluniversitypress.co.uk/journals/article/36384/`

The data shows that ideas, plans and decisions concerning childcare arrangements are highly gendered, being influenced by the expectations of partners, employers and colleagues and by a work culture which is not family friendly. Within this Italian social and political context, a mother-to-be faces a dilemma which is perceived as a private issue that concerns only herself. The new transition to parenthood forces women to redefine their identity and career in a highly gendered manner.

Source: Fortune (2024), qualitative reporting: `https://fortune.com/europe/2024/03/11/italian-women-eschewing-motherhood-italy-birth-rate-plunges-europes-lowest/`

"There is this idea of motherhood in Italy that can be unrealistic and charged with too much weight, whereas perhaps in some other countries it is not so all-encompassing," said Paola Marion, director of Italy's Psychoanalysis Review whose own patients are mainly women between the ages of 20 and 50. "If that becomes the reference model for young women, then it's hard to change with just policy actions."

"Mothers are required to give these children their absolute and total attention. If you think too hard about having kids you may simply give up."

**Social pressure from perceived societal expectations — survey data**

Source: ScienceDirect (2023), ISTAT-based survey on female labour market attachment: `https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537123000775`

55% of non-active females in the South (and 49% of active males in the South) are of the opinion that children suffer when the mother works, while only 30% of active females share this belief.

This differential (55% non-active vs 30% active women in the South) documents that social norms about motherhood function differently depending on a woman's own labour market position — a finding relevant to the judgment mechanism.

**Resignations after maternity — documented social and structural pressure**

Source: Save the Children "Le Equilibriste — La maternità in Italia 2022": `https://www.savethechildren.it/blog-notizie/le-equilibriste-la-maternita-in-italia-nel-2022`

In 2020 alone, more than 30,000 mothers resigned, often for family reasons, but also because they were not supported by local social services such as nurseries, which were either non-existent or too expensive.

72% of resignations in 2021 were submitted by women. Most of those who quit cited the difficulties associated with juggling work and childcare duties.

Source: Rome Business School / INAPP data (2025): `https://romebusinessschool.com/blog/women-and-work-in-italy/`

After maternity leave, 16% of women leave the workforce, compared to only 2.8% of men.

**Career break and social norms — individual preferences vs structural pressure (Italian Economic Journal 2025)**

Source: `https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40797-025-00332-7` — PLUS 2014 and 2021 surveys

This preference for parenting versus career is an individual characteristic which can be influenced by social norms and gender stereotypes. A decade ago, women characterised by higher parenting priority seemed to be more exposed to the risk of dropping out from the labour market; nowadays the desire to have kids appears to go side by side with the desire to maintain paid employment.

This finding documents a structural shift: women with high parenting priority are no longer more likely to leave the labour market than other women — suggesting that the perceived trade-off between motherhood and career is weakening, but the structural penalties remain.

**ISTAT data — half of women expect motherhood to worsen their work opportunities**

Source: ISTAT "Intenzioni di fecondità 2024" (documented and verified in previous task)

- Half of women think the arrival of a child would worsen their own work opportunities — verified direct quote from ISTAT 2024
- Among 18–24 year-old women this figure exceeds 65% — verified direct quote
- 59% of men do not anticipate any effect on their own situation — verified direct quote

This is the most directly relevant Italy-specific quantitative data for this task — and it comes from ISTAT primary data.

**Aassve et al. (2024) — parental pressure on non-normative choices (PDR)**

Source: verified in previous task from paper text

Italian parents tend to discourage non-normative behavior in their offspring, and even adult children feel themselves to be under pressure when making their own choices. Qualitative studies have clearly demonstrated that the widely prevailing parental pressure has discouraged cohabitation in a way that is more powerful than Catholic prescripts.

**State of Motherhood in Europe 2024 — Italy included**

Source: Make Mothers Matter / Kantar survey, N=9,600 mothers across 11 EU countries including Italy, 2024: `https://makemothersmatter.org/mmm-state-of-motherhood-in-europe-2024/`

Motherhood remains an under-explored and insufficiently addressed topic in both policy-making and societal discourse, leading to unmet needs and unseen challenges for mothers. With mounting pressure on modern women to "have it all", working mothers often struggle to reconcile career aspirations, caregiving roles and a sense of personal identity.

Note: Italy-specific disaggregated results from this survey are not available in the public extracts found — the full report requires direct access.

---

**Data gap acknowledged**

No Italian primary source survey was found that directly asks women: "Do you want children but feel socially judged for that desire?" The closest available instruments are: (1) the MatGuilt survey measuring the gap between personal and perceived societal ideals of motherhood; (2) the ISTAT intenzioni di fecondità data on perceived work impact; (3) the EVS/ISSP items on attitudes toward working mothers. A direct Eurispes survey on this specific question was not found in this session.
