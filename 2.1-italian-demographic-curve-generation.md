## Absolute Births and TFR by Period

- 1946–1948: post-war rebound — birth rate returns toward pre-war levels after wartime collapse [exact rate not verified from primary source]
- 1950–1963: reconstruction and economic miracle — birth rate maintained at elevated levels through the reconstruction period
- 1963–1965: peak of the Italian baby boom — 1964 was the single highest year with 1,016,120 live births and TFR 2.70; the peak should be understood as a three-year period 1963–1965, not a single year
- 1964–1976: rapid decline in TFR
- 1976: last year in which the replacement threshold of 2.1 was exceeded
- 1976–1995: structural decline — TFR falls from 2.1 to 1.19
- 1995: historical minimum of the period — TFR 1.19 children per woman
- 1995–2008: partial recovery — partly sustained by higher fertility rates among immigrant women
- 2008: peak of the 2000s recovery — 576,659 live births; birth rate 9.7 per thousand residents
- 2008–2024: uninterrupted decline for 16 consecutive years
- 2024: 369,944 live births — new absolute historical minimum since Italian Unification (1861); TFR 1.18; birth rate 6.3 per thousand residents
- January–July 2025 (provisional): 197,956 births — approximately 13,000 fewer than the same period in 2024, a decline of 6.3%; estimated TFR 1.13

---

## Structural Causes of the 2008–2024 Decline

### Source
ISTAT 2024

- The decline in births depends both on low propensity to have children (TFR 1.18 in 2024) and on the structural reduction in the number of potential parents — women born from the mid-1970s onward, when fertility began falling below 2 children per woman
- The decline in births from 2008 to 2024 is almost entirely attributable to the fall in births from couples where both parents are Italian, which constitute 78.2% of total births
- Births from couples where at least one parent is foreign (21.8% of total) remained substantially stable throughout the period

## Generational Cohort Mapping

| Generation | Birth years | Historical context |
|---|---|---|
| Silent Generation | 1928–1945 | War, Fascism, reconstruction |
| Baby Boomers | 1946–1964 | Reconstruction, economic miracle, peak births |
| Generation X | 1965–1980 | Anni di piombo, oil crises, first TFR decline |
| Millennials | 1981–1996 | TFR at historic low (1.19 in 1995), Tangentopoli, First Republic collapse |
| Generation Z | 1997–2012 | Great Recession, partial TFR recovery then renewed decline |
| Generation Alpha | 2013–present | TFR below 1.25, COVID, new historical minimums |
