# 1. Purpose and Methodology

## Objective

The report studies:

* **Personal values**
* **Identity dimensions**
* **Attitudes toward EU values (Article 2 TEU)**

It explores how these interact and vary across countries and socio-demographic groups.

## Methodology

* Survey conducted **Oct–Nov 2020**
* ~**27,000 respondents** across EU27
* Mixed methods:

  * Face-to-face (CAPI)
  * Online (CAWI) due to COVID-19
* Results are **population-weighted EU averages** 

---

# 2. Conceptual Framework

## Four Value Dimensions (Schwartz-inspired model)

1. **Self-transcendence**

   * Caring for others, equality, tolerance, nature

2. **Openness to change**

   * Autonomy, independent thinking, new experiences

3. **Conservation**

   * Security, tradition, conformity

4. **Self-enhancement**

   * Power, wealth, dominance

These form two axes:

* Conservation ↔ Openness
* Self-enhancement ↔ Self-transcendence 

---

# 3. Key Findings: Personal Values

## Most Important Values (EU average)

* Caring for close people (benevolence): **77–78%**
* Making own decisions (self-direction): **78%**
* Forming own opinions: **73%**
* Personal security: **72%**
* Caring for nature: **71%**
* Equal opportunities: **70%**

## Least Important Values

* Wealth: **13%**
* Power: **22%**
* New experiences: **47%** 

## Interpretation

* Europeans prioritize:

  * **Altruism + autonomy**
* They reject:

  * **Materialism + dominance**

---

## Aggregate Value Scores (EU level)

* **Self-transcendence: 80% (highest)**
* **Openness to change: 78%**
* **Conservation: 76%**
* **Self-enhancement: 45% (much lower)** 

### Insight

European societies are:

* Strongly **post-materialist**
* Balanced between **security and autonomy**
* Weakly oriented toward **power/wealth**

---

## Cross-country Patterns

* **Self-transcendence dominates** in most countries
* Exceptions:

  * **Bulgaria, Hungary → conservation highest**
  * **Denmark, Netherlands → openness highest**

### High conservation countries

* Greece, Bulgaria, Cyprus, Hungary (~84–86%)

### High self-enhancement countries

* Romania (63%)
* Italy (57%)
* Poland (56%) 

---

## Socio-demographic Patterns

### Age

* Young:

  * More **self-enhancement**
* Older:

  * More **conservation**

### Education

* Lower education:

  * More **conservation**
* Higher education:

  * More **openness**

### Political orientation

* Right:

  * More **conservation + self-enhancement**
* Left:

  * More **self-transcendence**

### Class

* Upper class:

  * More **self-enhancement**
* Working class:

  * More **conservation**

# 1. Purpose of the Paper

The article is a literature review of cross-national survey studies (1995–2023) on:

* Forms (meanings/contents) of national and European identity
* Their determinants (what shapes them)
* Their consequences (political and social effects)

## Core research problem

Most studies measure identity as intensity (attachment, belonging), but:

* They neglect what people actually mean by "national" or "European" identity.

---

# 2. Key Concept: "Forms" of Identity

"Forms" = the content and criteria people associate with belonging.

Examples:

* Being born in a country
* Respecting laws
* Sharing culture
* Having ancestry

---

# 3. Theoretical Framework

## 3.1 Social Identity Theory

Identity includes:

* **Cognitive** (knowledge of belonging)
* **Affective** (emotions)
* **Evaluative** (value attached)

## 3.2 Constructivist View

Identity is:

* Socially constructed
* Changeable
* Based on "imagined communities" (shared beliefs, not direct experience)

---

# 4. Main Types of National Identity

## 4.1 Ethnic Identity

Based on:

* Ancestry
* Birthplace
* Religion
* Heritage

**Characteristics:**

* Exclusive
* Ascriptive (you are born into it)

## 4.2 Civic Identity

Based on:

* Laws and institutions
* Citizenship
* Political values
* Participation

**Characteristics:**

* Inclusive
* Achievable (you can acquire it)

## 4.3 Cultural Identity

Based on:

* Language
* Traditions
* Customs
* History

## 4.4 Socioeconomic Identity (less studied)

Based on:

* Welfare system
* Economy
* Social protection

---

# 5. European Identity: Specific Forms

More clearly divided than national identity:

* **Civic European identity** → EU institutions, rights, citizenship
* **Cultural European identity** → shared history, civilization
* **Ethnic European identity** (emerging) → ancestry, religion, birthplace

# 7. Main Empirical Findings

## 7.1 Dominant Pattern

Most studies confirm:

* **Ethnic vs Civic dichotomy**

## 7.2 Stability Over Time

* **National identity > European identity** in strength
* **European identity:**
  * Stable from 1970–2013
  * No major increase or decline

---

# 8. Cross-Country Patterns

* **Eastern Europe:**
  * More ethnic / cultural identity
* **Western Europe:**
  * More civic identity

**BUT:**

* Evidence is inconsistent
* No universal pattern

---

# 9. Methodological Problems

## 9.1 Measurement Issues

* Same items interpreted differently across countries
* Example: language can be:
  * Civic OR cultural OR ethnic

## 9.2 Lack of Measurement Equivalence

* Cross-country comparisons are often unreliable

## 9.3 Conceptual Ambiguity

The "true national" question:

* Measures perceived group norms, not personal identity

## 9.4 Inconsistent Classification

Same variables assigned differently:

* Religion → ethnic OR cultural
* Language → civic OR cultural

---

# 10. Determinants (What Shapes Identity Forms)

## 10.1 Ethnic Identity increases with:

* Older age
* Religiosity
* Lower education
* Lower social status
* Less international exposure

## 10.2 Civic Identity:

* Influenced by similar factors (important insight)
* Also linked to:
  * Right-wing ideology (in some studies)
  * Higher education (mixed effects)

**Key insight:**

The same variables often strengthen BOTH ethnic and civic identity.

---

# 11. Consequences

## 11.1 Attitudes Toward Immigrants

**Ethnic identity:**
* Strongly linked to:
  * Anti-immigrant attitudes
  * Xenophobia
  * Negative views of Muslims

**Civic identity:**
* Mixed effects:
  * Sometimes reduces xenophobia
  * Sometimes increases it

## 11.2 Political Attitudes

**Civic identity:**
* More trust in institutions
* More support for EU integration

**Ethnic identity:**
* Less trust in institutions
* More nationalism and chauvinism
* Less support for EU

---

# 12. European Identity Outcomes

* **Civic European identity** → positive for cosmopolitanism and EU support
* **Cultural** → moderate positive
* **Ethnic** → negative

---

# 13. Key Paradox

Despite stable European identity:

* **Euroskepticism has increased**

**Explanation:**

* Studies ignore content of identity
* Different meanings lead to different political effects

---

# 14. New Typologies (Advanced Models)

Some studies identify types like:

* **Exclusionists** → all criteria important
* **Assimilationists** → high but reject ancestry/religion
* **Integrationists** → civic only
* **Pluralists** → reject all criteria

---

# 15. Main Conclusions

## 15.1 Core Findings

* Ethnic–civic distinction dominates research
* European identity = mostly civic vs cultural
* Cultural and socioeconomic aspects are underdeveloped

## 15.2 Major Problems

* Poor measurement tools
* Lack of comparability
* Overreliance on outdated surveys

---

# 16. Recommendations for Future Research

## Move beyond "criteria of belonging"

* Measure personal identification directly
* Add new dimensions
  * Cultural and socioeconomic identity

## Improve survey items

* Avoid ambiguous concepts (e.g., language)
* Separate values vs institutions
* Ideals vs real-world performance

## Use new methods

* Latent class analysis (person-centered)

---

# 17. Overall Takeaway

National and European identities are multidimensional, unstable, and poorly measured.

The field is dominated by a simplified ethnic vs civic dichotomy, but:

* **Reality is more complex**
* **Categories overlap**
* **Measurement tools distort findings**

**Understanding identity requires:**

* Better data
* More nuanced concepts
* Focus on meaning, not just intensity

Here is the summary with a clearer bullet-point structure:

**Topic and purpose**

* The article examines the development of **national identity in Italy** using empirical sociological data.
* It is part of a comparative research project conducted between the early 1980s and the mid-1990s across multiple countries.

**Methodology**

* Uses “integrated tests,” combining:

  * Open-ended self-descriptions
  * Structured questionnaires
* These tools aim to capture how individuals spontaneously define their identity and belonging.
* Focus is on identifying whether individuals express **national, regional, or supranational (e.g., European) identity**.

**Main findings (Italy vs other countries)**

* Between 1982 and 1993:

  * Italy shows **consistently low levels of national identity** (around 9% expressing national roles).
  * This is significantly lower than in countries like France, Spain, and Argentina.
* Regional identity in Italy is also weak and does not compensate for the lack of national identity.
* Supranational identity (European identity) is nearly absent in this period.

**Change over time**

* Around 1994, there is a **notable increase in national identity expressions** among Italians.
* The author interprets this as a **structural shift**, not just a temporary spike caused by external events (e.g., political or sports-related factors).

**Sociodemographic patterns**

* Individuals more likely to express national identity in Italy (earlier period):

  * Men more than women
  * Lower social strata more than middle classes
* Middle classes are underrepresented among those expressing national identity.

**Theoretical perspective**

* Identity is defined as a set of internal representations related to:

  * Social roles
  * Group belonging
  * Self-perception
* National identity is:

  * Socially constructed
  * Historically shaped
  * Continuously reproduced rather than fixed

**Overall conclusions**

* Italy historically had a relatively weak national identity compared to other countries.
* This weakness was not offset by regional or European identities.
* A significant increase in national identity occurs after the mid-1990s.
* National identity is treated as a dynamic and evolving sociological phenomenon rather than a static trait.

**Italy — National Identity: Data Summary**

**Source 1: Quaderni di Scienza Politica (longitudinal study, 1982–mid-1990s)**

- Between 1982 and 1993, only ~9% of Italians spontaneously expressed a national identity in integrated tests
- This figure is significantly lower than comparable countries in the same period (France, Spain, Argentina)
- Regional identity did not compensate — also weak
- Supranational (European) identity was nearly absent in this period
- Sociodemographic pattern: national identity was more present among men and lower social strata; middle classes were underrepresented
- Around 1994, a notable and structural increase in national identity expressions was recorded
- The author treats this as a durable shift, not a contingent spike

**Source 2: Special Eurobarometer 508 (fieldwork Oct–Nov 2020, N~27,000 EU27)**

- EU average: family 81%, nationality 73%, gender 72% as most important identity dimensions
- Italy-specific disaggregated data on national identity ranking not available in this document
- Italy scores 57% on self-enhancement (power, wealth, dominance) — among the highest in the EU, alongside Romania (63%) and Poland (56%)
- EU average for self-enhancement is 45%
- High conservation countries include Greece, Bulgaria, Cyprus, Hungary (84–86%); Italy is not in this group

**Source 3: Literature review on forms of national and European identity (studies 1995–2023)**

- European identity has remained stable in intensity from 1970 to 2013 — no major increase or decline
- Eastern Europe tends toward ethnic/cultural identity; Western Europe toward civic identity — but evidence is inconsistent
- No Italy-specific data in this document
- Key methodological warning: cross-national comparisons on identity are often unreliable due to different interpretations of the same survey items across countries

**General methodological note**

- The dominant measurement tool (the "true national" question) measures perceived group norms, not personal identification directly
- Same variables (e.g. language, religion) are classified differently across studies, limiting comparability
