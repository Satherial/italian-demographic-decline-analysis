## East Asian Comparison: Work Culture as the Primary Barrier to Parenthood

- Establish the centrality of work culture as a significant deterrent to family formation in Japan. Government and academic analyses identify "lack of time" as a key cause of declining birth rates, with chronic time scarcity for dual-income households juggling work, housework, and childcare leading many to abandon plans for a second or third child. Japanese annual working hours stood at 1,617 hours in 2024, above the United Kingdom (1,512 hours), France (1,491 hours), and Germany (1,331 hours). Since 1990, Japan has experienced a reduction in working hours from 2,031 hours annually, driven by the five-day workweek expansion and the 1994 Labor Standards Act amendment establishing the 40-hour week.

- Document the phenomenon of *karoshi* (death from overwork) as a cultural indicator of Japan's work-centric social structure. The term reflects a societal reality where work obligations systematically override personal life. Workplace stigma deters employees—particularly men—from taking parental leave, with 17.13% of eligible men taking paternity leave in fiscal year 2022, compared to 80.2% of women. The Japanese government has set targets to increase this rate to 50% by fiscal 2025 and 85% by fiscal 2030, with companies now required to disclose their paternity leave rates. In 2024, births to Japanese nationals fell to 686,061, the lowest on record, while the total fertility rate dropped to approximately 1.1.

- Analyze South Korea's working hour reduction trends and their relationship to fertility policy. Over the past decade, average weekday primary working hours in South Korea decreased by approximately 20%, from 320.2 minutes (5.3 hours) to 257.5 minutes (4.3 hours), while time spent socializing with family and friends increased from 35 minutes to 102.2 minutes on weekdays. The 2023 average annual working hours stood at 1,874 hours, down from 2,071 hours in 2013, though still 155 hours longer than the OECD average of 1,719 hours. The Korea Labor Institute attributes these reductions to the widespread adoption of the five-day workweek and the gradual implementation of working hour reduction policies.

- Document the recent South Korean fertility trend. The total fertility rate rose marginally from 0.72 in 2023 to 0.75 in 2024, with live births increasing by 8,300 (3.6%) to 238,300. This period saw comprehensive policy interventions including the "6+6 Parental Leave System" implemented in January 2024, which provides 100% wage replacement for the first six months when both parents take leave. Male parental leave applicants exceeded 60,000 for the first time in 2024, representing 29.2% of all parental leave takers, a dramatic increase of 18.3% from the previous year. The National Assembly Budget Office reports that the budget for work-family balance measures increased from 1.9 trillion won in 2020 to 4.6 trillion won in 2025.

- Examine the persistent gender gap in unpaid work as a common barrier across East Asia. In South Korea, women's weekday childcare time decreased from 44.7 minutes to 28.4 minutes over the past decade, while men's increased only marginally from 9.4 minutes to 9.9 minutes. The primary responsibility for caregiving remains concentrated among women despite increased male participation in parental leave. OECD data confirms that across member countries, women work on average longer per day than men when both paid and unpaid work are considered, with significant gender gaps observed in Italy, Spain, Estonia, Greece, and Hungary.

- Contrast the distinct cultural logic of East Asian fertility decline with the Italian pattern using time-use data. Full-time employed workers in Japan have approximately 14 hours of time off daily, while Italian workers enjoy roughly 16.5 hours—a difference that accumulates to significant annual time disparity. This inverse relationship—more leisure time in Italy correlating with fertility decline, less leisure time in East Asia also correlating with fertility decline—reveals distinct mechanisms: Italians may prioritize existing leisure; East Asians may lack sufficient leisure to consider parenthood.

- Analyze the OECD's framing of work-life balance as a demographic determinant. The OECD emphasizes that "key economic variables, such as household income, how it is split between parents, the cost of childcare and housing, can all affect whether to have children, when to have them, and how many children a family may have" . The organization has explicitly attributed South Korea's low fertility to a "challenging social climate that makes it difficult to balance work and family," recommending working hour reductions to improve work-life balance .

- Document the Italian exception to East Asian patterns: in Italy, cultural prioritization of lifestyle and leisure versus the East Asian time scarcity. Italian cultural values may prioritize *dolce vita*—sociality, domestic comfort, and time for self—representing a rejection of sacrifice for its own sake. In contrast, East Asian rejection of parenthood is more accurately characterized as an inability to add parenthood to already overburdened lives. As Japanese policy analysis states: "For dual-income households juggling work, housework, and childcare all at once, the lack of time on weekdays becomes chronic, often leading them to give up on having a second or third child".

- Include the structural barriers to parental leave in Japan despite legal protections. The Child Care and Family Care Leave Law stipulates that employers cannot refuse child care leave requests, but workers employed for less than one year can be exempted under labor-management agreements . Many fathers cite workplace pressure, career advancement timing, and concerns about burdening colleagues as reasons for not taking leave. One surveyed employee stated: "If doing so would cause problems for others, it's better for me to manage the job myself" . This workplace culture creates a structural barrier that policy alone has struggled to overcome.

- Compare the divergent policy trajectories and their demographic outcomes. While South Korea has recently shown marginal fertility improvement following comprehensive work-family balance policies, Japan continues to see declines with births falling below 700,000 in 2024—more than a decade earlier than previously projected. The OECD notes that Germany improved fertility rates through "parental allowance" and "parental time" policies in the 2000s, which "led to society pushing men to spend time with their children and helped change their thinking about child rearing." This suggests that cultural change, not merely policy implementation, is important for sustained demographic recovery, alongside factors like immigration and socioeconomic changes.
