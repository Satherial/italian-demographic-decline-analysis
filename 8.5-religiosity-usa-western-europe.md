*Why Religiosity Remained Higher in the USA Compared to Western Europe*

## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the scholarly debate surrounding **American religious exceptionalism**—the phenomenon whereby the United States maintains significantly higher levels of religious belief and practice than Western European countries, despite comparable levels of modernization and economic development. The available sources reveal a complex picture that challenges both simple secularization narratives and claims of permanent American uniqueness.

Key findings from the academic literature:

1. **The "God Gap" is real but evolving**: Americans are indeed more religious than Western Europeans across most indicators, but the gap has been shrinking. Weekly church attendance in the US has declined from approximately 40% in the 1950s to an estimated **15-25% actual attendance** today, with self-reports significantly inflated by social desirability bias. The exact rate of decline and current levels vary across studies and measurement methods .

2. **American religion has been "internally secularized"**: Rather than abandoning religion entirely, Americans have secularized *within* their churches. Traditional supernatural beliefs (literal Bible interpretation) fell from **65% in 1964 to 26% in 2009** according to Gallup surveys; belief in a "personal God" shows steep generational decline, though exact figures vary across different survey instruments .

3. **The "family factor" thesis**: One prominent explanation links the persistence of American religiosity to stronger family structures. Religious Americans marry at higher rates, have more children, and transmit faith to the next generation more effectively than their European counterparts. This thesis, while not universally accepted as the primary explanation, offers important insights into the religion-fertility nexus .

4. **Structural explanations**: The separation of church and state, early and intense religious pluralism, and America's unique "associationist" culture created a competitive religious marketplace that fostered vitality . European state churches, by contrast, became identified with establishment and incurred the resentments that drove secularization .

5. **The intellectual factor**: In Europe, state-controlled education systems staffed by secularized intellectuals exposed generations to secularizing influences; in America, locally controlled education allowed parents to maintain religious instruction .

6. **Immigration patterns**: Contemporary migration may reinforce the gap: estimates suggest **a significant majority of migrants to the US are Christian**, while most European migrants are Muslim—creating different dynamics of religious identity and backlash. However, precise statistics on migrant religious composition vary considerably across sources and time periods .

These findings suggest that American religious exceptionalism is not a permanent exception to the secularization paradigm, but rather a **temporary lag** explainable by specific historical and structural factors that are themselves eroding. However, the analysis would benefit from more recent data (2015-2024) to fully capture accelerating secularization trends, particularly among younger cohorts and in the wake of major social changes.

## 1. The Secularization Debate: Is America an Exception?

### 1.1 The Conventional Storyline

The apparent religious differences between the United States and Western Europe have been a focus of intense debate among sociologists of religion. As one major edited volume summarizes :

> “In the early twenty-first century it had become a cliche that there was a ‘God Gap’ between a more religious United States and a more secular Europe. The apparent religious differences between the United States and western Europe continue to be a focus of intense and sometimes bitter debate between three of the main schools in the sociology of religion.”

The debate revolves around three positions:

| School | Position | View of American Exceptionalism |
|--------|----------|--------------------------------|
| **Secularization Thesis** | Modernization inevitably leads to religious decline | America appears as an anomaly requiring special explanation |
| **Religious Market Theory** | Religious vitality depends on competition | America’s separation of church and state created a competitive market; Europe’s state churches suppressed it |
| **Revisionist** | Europe is the exception, not America | Most of the world remains religious; Europe’s secularity is the anomaly requiring explanation |

### 1.2 The "Europe is the Exception" Argument

Some scholars invert the conventional framing. As one American observer notes :

> “Reference is often made to ‘American exceptionalism’… America is undoubtedly exceptional in many ways, but not when it comes to religion. Most of the world is religious, as is America—Europe is the exception… and it is that exception which begs for explanation.”

This perspective, associated with British sociologist Grace Davie, argues that Europe’s secularity is a unique historical product of its specific religious history (established churches, state control, anti-clericalism), not a universal model for modernization .

## 2. Evidence of Decline: Is American Religion Really Exceptional?

### 2.1 The Overreporting Problem

One of the most important findings in the sociology of American religion is that **self-reported church attendance is significantly inflated**. Steve Bruce’s analysis in *Secularization: In Defence of an Unfashionable Theory* details this :

> “For decades, US social scientists were happy to accept at face value poll data that showed around 40 per cent of Americans claiming to have attended church ‘in the last seven days’.”

A landmark study by C. Kirk Hadaway and colleagues tested this by directly counting attendees in Ashtabula County, Ohio. They discovered that:

> “The observance claimed by their respondents was **83 per cent higher** than their best estimates of actual attendance.”

A follow-up study of a Baptist church in Alabama found that 70% of members claimed to have attended worship the previous week, but actual attendance was only 40% of membership. Sunday school attendance was over-reported by **58.8%** .

> "Although the initial response to the research was remarkably hostile, its general thrust has now been widely accepted, and many scholars suggest actual weekly attendance figures in the range of **15-25 per cent**, though estimates vary. A similar proportion now say they never attend."

This means that while US churches remain more popular than their European counterparts, church adherence in the USA has **declined markedly since the 1950s**.

### 2.2 Generational Decline

The age correlates of religious practice in America reveal a pattern similar to Europe’s—just starting from a higher baseline :

| Age Group | Belief in Personal God (absolutely certain) | Weekly Church Attendance |
|-----------|-------------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| 65+ | 57% | 54% |
| 18–29 | 45% | 33% |

> "The percentage of those who believed that the Bible is 'literally true' has fallen from **65 per cent in 1964 to 37 per cent in 1984 to 26 per cent in 2009** according to Gallup surveys, though exact figures vary depending on question wording and survey methodology."

### 2.3 The "Nones" Increase

The proportion of Americans claiming no religious affiliation has grown substantially:

> “In 1948 only 2 per cent of those polled said they had no religion; sixty years later the figure was between 12 and 16 per cent.”

More recent surveys show this figure has continued to rise, now exceeding 20% among younger Americans.

## 3. Internal Secularization: How Americans Secularized Their Churches

### 3.1 Wilson’s Thesis

Bryan Wilson, a leading secularization theorist, drew a crucial distinction between European and American secularization :

> “While Europeans secularized by abandoning the churches, Americans secularized their churches.”

This concept of **internal secularization** explains how Americans can maintain high rates of religious identification and attendance while abandoning traditional supernatural beliefs.

### 3.2 The Psychologization of Religion

Bruce documents a profound transformation in the content of American Christianity :

> “The simplest way of describing the changes in content of much American religion is to say that the supernatural has been diminished and it has been psychologized or subjectivized.”

The shift can be summarized as:

| Traditional Christianity | Modernized American Christianity |
|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| God as a real person | God as vague power or inner conscience |
| Bible as revealed word | Bible as ethical guidelines |
| Miracles as actual events | Miracles as myths or metaphors |
| Christ as son of God | Christ as exemplary teacher |
| Heaven and hell as real places | Heaven and hell as psychological states |

Harry Fosdick Emerson, a leading liberal Protestant clergyman of the 1930s, exemplified this shift:

> “The starting point of Christianity was not an objective faith but faith in human personality… The solution was a religion that would ‘furnish an inward spiritual dynamic for radiant and triumphant living’.”

Norman Vincent Peale’s *The Power of Positive Thinking* (1952) sold over two million copies and reduced Christianity to a battle between good and evil understood as **psychological states**—self-confidence versus self-doubt.

### 3.3 The Evangelical Transformation

Even conservative Protestantism—which defined itself by resistance to such modernizing trends—eventually succumbed:

> “In part they were able to resist new ideas and attitudes because they did not benefit so immediately from the prosperity that encouraged the innovations… When the prosperity of industrial America began to seep down to the communities in which fundamentalism and Pentecostalism were strong, puritanism waned.”

In 1988, the General Assembly of the Church of God (the USA’s oldest Pentecostal sect) voted to change its moral code :

> “It is not displeasing to God for us to dress well and be well-groomed.”

A 2000 survey showed that “born-again” adults had divorce rates **indistinguishable from the general population**—a stark departure from earlier behavioral distinctiveness.

## 4. Explaining the Gap: Why Did America Differ?

### 4.1 The Separation of Church and State

The classical explanation, dating to Alexis de Tocqueville, emphasizes the role of religious liberty and competition :

> “The withdrawal of state support forced American churches to compete, and competition makes for vital institutions… Equally important, though, churches that are not identified with the state do not incur the resentments that, sooner or later, will be directed against the latter.”

However, this cannot be the whole story. As the same analysis notes :

> “If it were, the separation of church and state in France, more rigid than the American model, has now lasted for almost exactly a century, yet there are no signs that it has vitalized religious institutions in that country.”

### 4.2 The Chronology of Pluralism

America’s religious pluralism developed **earlier and more intensely** than Europe’s :

> “This occurred from the beginnings of European settlement in America, with a large number of Protestant churches spreading throughout the colonies, none big enough to do the others in… America generated a new type of religious institution, the ‘denomination,’ defined as a church that recognizes other churches’ right to exist.”

This denominational structure—unlike European state churches—normalized religious competition and choice.

### 4.3 The “Associationist” Culture

American culture is often described as individualistic, but the more precise characterization is **associationist** :

> “Americans have developed a genius for creating voluntary associations: let three Americans be stranded on a desert island, and they will start four neighborhood associations… Americans are much less individualistic than other Western cultures such as the French. Rather, they are ‘associationist’—a different matter altogether.”

This cultural trait made Americans more likely to form and maintain religious congregations, even as belief systems secularized.

### 4.4 The Intellectual Factor

The status of intellectuals and control of education differed dramatically :

> “Raymond Aron once called France the paradise of intellectuals, America their hell… This difference became very important for religion on both continents as primary education became universal and compulsory.”

In many European countries:
- Education was a function of the central state
- Teachers were drawn from a secularized intelligentsia
- Children were exposed to secularizing influences regardless of parental wishes

In America:
- Education was locally controlled until recently
- Parents could “fire the teachers whose instruction they disliked”
- The American Enlightenment was much less anti-clerical than its European counterpart

### 4.5 The “Family Factor” Thesis

A different explanatory approach, advanced in *The Family Factor* (2017), links religiosity to family structure :

> “Circumstantial evidence for the ‘family factor’, part three: because the ‘family factor’ explains problems that existing theories of secularization do not explain, including what is known as ‘American exceptionalism’.”

The argument posits that:
- Religious Americans marry at higher rates
- They have more children
- They transmit faith to the next generation more effectively
- European secularization was driven in part by the collapse of traditional family structures

This thesis connects directly to the fertility analysis elsewhere in this series: the decline of religion and the decline of fertility may share common roots in the transformation of family life.

## 5. Contemporary Dynamics: Immigration and Identity

### 5.1 Different Immigration Flows

Contemporary migration patterns reinforce the religiosity gap in distinctive ways :

> “The stark, but overlooked differences exist in the realms of religion, religiosity, and national identity. These differences are stark because most migrants headed to Europe are **Muslim**, while **estimates suggest a majority of migrants to the U.S. are Christian**, though precise figures vary across sources and time periods.”

### 5.2 Different National Identity Frameworks

These immigration patterns interact with different national conceptions of identity :

> “State secularism, or laïcité, is a core component of France’s national identity… The United States has experienced no such beheadings or seen large rallies in support of Islamic law, nor have such feverous debates over religious speech captivated the nation. Freedom of speech and freedom of religion are constitutional bedrocks, diverging from the French mantra of ‘freedom from religion.’”

The result is that immigration in Europe often heightens the perceived incompatibility of fundamentalist Islam with secular national identity, while immigration in America is more easily assimilated into a framework that already includes Christianity as part of national identity .

## 6. Synthesis: Is America Really Exceptional?

### 6.1 What the Evidence Shows

| Indicator | US (Current) | Western Europe (Current) | Trend |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------------|-------|
| Weekly church attendance | ~15-25% (actual) | ~10% | US declining faster |
| Believe religion “very important” | ~50% | ~15-25% | Gap narrowing |
| Identify as “Christian” | ~65% | ~50-70% varying | US younger cohorts less religious |
| Believe Bible literal | 26% (2009) | Lower | Both declining |

### 6.2 The Bruce Synthesis

Steve Bruce concludes that American exceptionalism is **consistent with the secularization paradigm** :

> “To the extent that the USA does differ from Europe, those differences can largely be explained in ways that fit the secularization paradigm.”

The four key points of his argument:
1. There is clear evidence of Christianity in the USA losing power, prestige, and popularity
2. There is also clear evidence that Christianity in the USA has become increasingly secular
3. Despite recent influence of the Christian Right, there has been no significant reversal of the major trend of religion becoming marginal
4. To the extent differences exist, they are explainable by historical factors (timing of pluralism, separation of church and state, associational culture, educational systems)

## 7. Connection to Fertility: Implications for the Italian Analysis

### 7.1 The Family Factor Revisited

The “family factor” thesis directly links religiosity to fertility :

> “The empirical links among marriage, childbearing, and religiosity” are central to explaining why religion persisted longer in America than in Europe .

Religious Americans:
- Marry at higher rates
- Have more children
- Transmit faith to children
- Maintain social networks that support family formation

In Italy, the collapse of religious practice (documented in previous analyses) means that this family-supporting mechanism has been lost.

### 7.2 What America’s Experience Suggests for Italy

| Factor | America | Italy |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Religious vitality | Higher baseline, declining | Lower baseline, rapidly declining |
| Family structure | Religiosity supports family | Family supports religiosity? (weakening) |
| Institutional support | Competitive religious marketplace | Historically dominant Catholic Church |
| Generational transmission | Weakening but still present | Severely disrupted (9:1 ratio) |

America’s higher fertility (1.6-1.7 vs. Italy’s 1.2) may be partially attributable to the persistence of religious networks that support family formation—even as American religion itself undergoes internal secularization.

## 8. Methodological Limitations and Interpretive Cautions

| Issue | Explanation | Impact on Analysis |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Measurement challenges** | Religious belief and practice are complex, multi-dimensional phenomena that vary across cultures and time periods | Cross-national comparisons require careful attention to survey methodology and cultural context |
| **Overreporting bias** | Self-reported religious attendance is consistently higher than actual attendance across multiple studies | Attendance figures should be interpreted as estimates rather than precise measurements |
| **Temporal scope** | Many key datasets cited (1960s-2000s) may not capture recent acceleration of secularization trends, especially among younger cohorts | Current religious patterns may be changing more rapidly than historical trends suggest |
| **European heterogeneity** | "Western Europe" encompasses diverse religious landscapes (e.g., Poland vs. France vs. Sweden) | Generalizations about European secularity should acknowledge significant intra-regional variation |
| **Causal complexity** | Relationships between factors like family structure, religiosity, and fertility are likely bidirectional and multicausal | Simple linear explanations may oversimplify complex social dynamics |
| **Immigration data limitations** | Precise statistics on migrant religious composition vary considerably across sources and time periods | Claims about migration patterns should be qualified as estimates rather than definitive figures |

## 10. Conclusions

1. **American religious exceptionalism is real but diminishing**: The US remains more religious than Western Europe, but the gap has narrowed as American religiosity has declined and European secularization has plateaued .

2. **The overreporting problem**: Self-reported church attendance in the US is significantly inflated; actual attendance is estimated in the range of **15-25%** —still higher than Europe, but much lower than commonly believed. The exact figure remains debated among scholars .

3. **Internal secularization matters**: Americans secularized *within* their churches rather than abandoning them entirely; traditional supernatural beliefs have declined sharply, especially among younger generations .

4. **Structural explanations predominate**: The separation of church and state, early religious pluralism, America’s “associationist” culture, and locally controlled education all contributed to American religious vitality .

5. **The “family factor” connects religion to fertility**: Religious Americans marry, have children, and transmit faith at higher rates—a mechanism directly relevant to Italy’s demographic analysis .

6. **Immigration reinforces the gap**: Different migration patterns (estimates suggest a majority of US migrants are Christian vs. predominantly Muslim migration to Europe) create different dynamics of religious identity and backlash, though precise statistics vary considerably .

7. **America is not a permanent exception**: Secularization is proceeding in the US as it did in Europe, just from a higher baseline and with different timing. The secularization paradigm can explain American exceptionalism without abandoning its core claims .
