## Israel

**Sources**: Taub Center for Social Policy Studies in Israel, Demography Overview 2024: `https://www.taubcenter.org.il/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/Demography-2024-ENG-1.pdf` — Taub Center "Israel's Exceptional Fertility": `https://www.taubcenter.org.il/en/research/israels-exceptional-fertility/` — Jewish People Policy Institute 2024 — CBS (Israel Central Bureau of Statistics) 2024

### TFR Trend

- Israel is the **only OECD country that has not followed the Second Demographic Transition**
- TFR has **never dropped below 2.8 children per woman**
- TFR 2015–2018: approximately **3.10**
- TFR 2023: **2.84**
- TFR 2024: provisional data only — exact verified figure not available; situation complicated by ongoing war since October 2023. The figure 2.91 cited in the previous version is removed as unverified

### Subgroup Breakdown (2022, CBS)

- **Haredi (Ultra-Orthodox) Jewish**: TFR approximately **6.4–6.6**
- **Non-Haredi Jewish**: TFR approximately **2.4–2.5**
- **Secular and traditional Jewish women** (self-identified): TFR exceeds **2.2**
- **Muslim**: TFR approximately **2.71**
- **Christian**: TFR approximately **1.61**
- **Druze**: TFR approximately **1.64**

### Key Structural Observations (Taub Center, verified)

- **Strong pronatalist norms** cut across all educational levels and degrees of religiosity in the Jewish population
- **Fertility has been increasing alongside increasing age at first birth and increasing female education** — a pattern atypical in international perspective
- **Factors typically associated with fertility suppression elsewhere** — high housing costs, limited public childcare funding, delayed childbearing, high female labor force participation — apply in Israel as much as in other OECD countries but do not produce equivalent fertility decline
- **College-educated Israeli women do not have fewer children than less-educated peers** — the opposite of the international pattern

## South Korea — Pre-Decline and Collapse

**Sources**: Georgetown Journal of International Affairs (2024): `https://gjia.georgetown.edu/2024/09/24/the-necessary-paradigm-shift-for-south-koreas-ultra-low-fertility/` — PMC (2024): `https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11884948/` — The Diplomat (2024): `https://thediplomat.com/2024/09/a-population-expert-deciphers-south-koreas-plummeting-birth-rate/`

### TFR Historical Series

- **1960**: TFR approximately **6.0**
- **1983**: TFR **2.06** — first drop below replacement level
- **2002**: TFR **1.17** — below the 1.3 "lowest-low fertility" threshold
- **2018**: TFR **0.98** — first and only OECD country to fall below 1.0
- **2023**: TFR **0.72**
- **2024**: TFR **0.75** (provisional, Wikipedia citing CBS Korea)

### Historical Policy Context

- From **1961**, the South Korean government initiated a family planning program advocating for a "small and prosperous family" to support rapid economic industrialization
- By **1964**, IUD adoption was widespread, reflecting mass acceptance of smaller families
- **The cultural shift toward small families, engineered by state policy for economic purposes, proved impossible to reverse once prosperity arrived**
- South Korea has spent approximately **$270 billion over 18 years** on pronatalist policies including baby bonuses and cash transfers — births continue to decline

### Attitudinal Data (The Diplomat 2024)

- **81.7% of Koreans** agree with the statement "being a parent is an invaluable thing in life," including **67.0% of people in their 20s**
- **All age groups** report considering two children as the ideal family size
- In practice, younger Koreans use the term **"sam-po generation"** — referring to the renunciation of dating, marriage, and children as three things they must give up

### Demographic Mechanism

- South Korea is identified by demographers as having fallen into a **"low fertility trap"**: when birth rates are low for more than one generation, small or no family formation becomes the new social norm, replacing the previous social signal that family formation is normal and expected

## United States

**Sources**: CDC NCHS National Vital Statistics Report 2023 (published March 2025): `https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data/nvsr/nvsr74/nvsr74-1.pdf` — CDC Provisional Data 2024: `https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data/vsrr/vsrr038.pdf` — Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health (2026): `https://publichealth.jhu.edu/2026/is-the-us-birth-rate-declining`

### TFR Trend

- **Peak during baby boom**: approximately **3.5** in the early 1960s
- **Below replacement level generally since 1971**
- **Consistently below replacement since 2008**
- **2007**: TFR **2.1** — last year at replacement level
- **2008–2024**: decline in almost every year following the Great Recession
- **2023**: TFR **1.621** (CDC NVSR, verified)
- **2024**: TFR **1.599–1.63** depending on source and methodology (CDC provisional data, Child Trends) — new historical minimum

### Note on Previous Version Error

- The figure **1.79** cited as World Bank 2024 data in the previous version was incorrect — removed. The correct CDC provisional figure for 2024 is **1.599**. The 1.79 figure likely refers to an earlier year or a different calculation method.

### Breakdown by Group (CDC NVSR 2023)

- **TFR was below replacement for all race and Hispanic-origin groups in 2023**
- **Exception**: Native Hawaiian and Other Pacific Islander women
- **White non-Hispanic TFR declined 2% in 2023 versus 2022** — well below replacement level
- **The decline in TFR is documented across all demographic groups**

### Policy Context

- **Pronatalism is emerging as a political theme** in the United States as of 2024–2025
- **The Trump administration** has publicly stated it is seeking policy ideas to encourage Americans to have more children
- **A 2013 Gallup survey** found American adults on average want 2–3 children; stated desired family size has remained relatively stable while realized fertility has declined

## Correction to Original Hypothesis

The original research framework framed the USA as a country where "the white Christian population maintains demographic growth." The CDC data does not support this framing as of 2023–2024. **TFR is below replacement for all racial and ethnic groups including White non-Hispanic**. The USA TFR hit a new historical minimum in 2024. This factual correction should be incorporated into the analysis before using the USA as a comparative case.
