## Fertility Among Italian Social Classes by Income Quintiles

### Methodological Caveat: What Istat Actually Publishes

Before proceeding to the substantive analysis, a fundamental clarification is required.
**Istat does not publish a direct TFR-by-income-quintile table as a standalone statistical product.** The main demographic portal (*demo.istat.it*) disaggregates fertility rates by age, region, citizenship, and birth order—but not by household income quintile. The quintile breakdowns in Istat publications (drawn from the EU-SILC / IT-SILC longitudinal survey) concern income distribution and poverty risk, rather than specific fertility outcomes per quintile.

The analysis below therefore proceeds through three legitimate proxies:

1.  **Education level** – The most systematically studied proxy for social class in Italian fertility research, utilizing Istat's *Famiglia e Soggetti Sociali* (FSS) multipurpose surveys.
2.  **Employment stability** – The most operationally precise class indicator for reproductive-age adults in the Italian dual labor market.
3.  **Income level** – Directly measured in recent studies using longitudinal tax data and cross-country panel surveys, allowing for genuine income-fertility analysis.

---

### 1. The Education Gradient: Negative and Intensifying

The most documented dimension of the social class–fertility relationship in Italy uses educational attainment as a proxy for status. Key findings from **Tanturri, Manganelli & Impicciatore (2016)**, using Istat FSS data, show that education remains a primary driver of fertility behavior, with its relevance increasing among younger cohorts.

The structural finding is a **negative association between female educational attainment and fertility**:

* A higher proportion of highly educated women postpone first childbirth or remain permanently childless.
* **The "Time-Squeeze" Effect:** Among those who do become mothers, higher education correlates with a *higher* conditional probability of having a second child. Highly educated women who enter motherhood tend to "catch up," resulting in a bimodal distribution: either no children at all or a two-child family.

This contradicts the "poverty causes low fertility" narrative. **The social strata with the highest human capital are systematically the most likely to remain childless or to delay childbirth.** According to **Skirbekk (2008)**, while high-income groups historically moved from high to low fertility, education has acted as a persistent depressor of fertility throughout the 20th and 21st centuries.

---

### 2. The Employment-Stability Gradient: The Sharpest Class Divide

The most specific indicator of social class for reproductive-age Italians is employment stability. Italy's labor market dualization, beginning in the late 1990s, concentrated precarious contracts on younger cohorts (ages 25–35).

**Barbieri, Bozzon, Scherer, Grotti & Lugo (2015)** demonstrated that this "Latin Model" of deregulation at the margins significantly reduces the probability of transitioning to motherhood. The lack of institutional buffers in Italy makes employment instability a far stronger deterrent to fertility than in Northern Europe.

The most recent evidence from **Alderotti, Guetto, Barbieri, Scherer & Vignoli (2025)** confirms:
* **Career Fragmentation:** The accumulation of precarious job spells reduces completed fertility more severely than single episodes of unemployment.
* **Rising Childlessness:** Using FSS 2016 data, the study shows female childlessness rose from **13.94%** (1951–55 cohort) to **24.45%** (1971–75 cohort). For men, it rose from **16.71%** to **31.27%** in the same period.

The class interpretation is direct: **Permanent contract = Insider class = Higher fertility; Precarious contract = Outsider class = Lower fertility.**

---

### 3. The Income Gradient: Positive and Increasingly Pronounced

Recent longitudinal data challenges the assumption that the "poor have more children." In contemporary Italy, the income gradient for fertility is **positive and steepening.**

**Gil-Hernández, Vignoli, Guetto, Maitino & Ravagli (2025)**, using longitudinal tax data from Tuscany (2003–2021), found:
* Higher earnings for **both** men and women increase the likelihood of a first birth.
* Couples where both partners are high earners are the *most* likely to have children, while low-income couples are the *least* likely.

**Van Wijk & Billari (2024)** argue that the economic prerequisites for parenthood have escalated. It is not just "objective" poverty, but the **subjective income floor**—the level of income perceived as necessary to raise a child responsibly—that has risen. This affects lower quintiles disproportionately, as their income fails to meet these rising "entry costs" for parenthood, which often include securing **homeownership** as a fundamental milestone before childbearing.

---

### 4. Poverty and Family Size: The Istat Data on Risk

According to **Istat EU-SILC 2024** data (*Condizioni di vita e reddito delle famiglie*), the relationship between income and family size reveals a "poverty trap" rather than high fertility among the poor:

* **Poverty Risk by Family Type:** The risk of poverty or social exclusion is lowest for childless couples and remains contained (approx. **19%**) for couples with 1 or 2 children.
* **The Large Family Penalty:** The risk jumps to **34.8%** for couples with 3 or more children.

This suggests that large families in Italy often *become* poor after childbirth due to inadequate welfare. Furthermore, **Bank of Italy (2021)** research by Bovini & Colonna estimates the average monthly direct cost of a minor child at **€640–€645**. While costs vary by region (higher in the North), this high fixed cost acts as a barrier for lower-income quintiles.

---

### Synthesis: The "Structural Bind" of the Middle Class

| Dimension | Direction | Strongest Effect |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Education (Female)** | Negative | High childlessness in top educational quartile |
| **Employment Stability** | Strongly Positive | Permanent contracts act as a "gatekeeper" to fertility |
| **Income Level** | Positive | High-earner couples are the most fertile |
| **Poverty Risk** | Positive | Large families (3+ children) face the highest poverty risk |

**Conclusion:** In Italy, low income prevents crossing the rising economic threshold for parenthood, while high education increases the opportunity costs and delay. The middle quintiles are caught in a "structural bind": they possess the aspirations for parenthood but lack the institutional support (housing, stable jobs, childcare) to reach the necessary economic prerequisites during their optimal reproductive years.
