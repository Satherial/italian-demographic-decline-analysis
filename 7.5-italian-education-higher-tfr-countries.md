## Executive Summary

This analysis examines the structural characteristics of Italy's education system—specifically the **extended duration of tertiary education and the age at which students obtain master's degrees**—in comparison with countries that maintain higher total fertility rates (TFR). The available OECD data reveal a consistent pattern: countries with **shorter, more streamlined educational pathways** and **earlier labor market entry** tend to have higher fertility rates, while countries with **extended educational trajectories** and **later graduation ages** show lower fertility.

Key findings from OECD *Education at a Glance 2025* and complementary sources:

1. **Italy's tertiary attainment rate among 25‑34 year‑olds is 28.27%** , placing it among the lowest in the OECD and significantly below the OECD average of 48% . This paradoxically low attainment rate—combined with Italy's very low TFR (1.2)—suggests that the *structure* and *timing* of education matter more than simply the *level* of attainment.

2. **Finland**, with a TFR of approximately 1.4 (higher than Italy), has a master's degree attainment rate among 25‑34 year‑olds of **15%** (slightly below the OECD average of 16%) but a distinctive feature: **late enrollment ages and widespread "gap year" practices** . Despite this, Finland maintains higher fertility than Italy, suggesting that other factors—particularly gender equality and institutional support—mediate the education‑fertility relationship.

3. **Mexico**, with a TFR of **1.9** (well above the OECD average), has a tertiary attainment rate of only **27.06%** among 25‑34 year‑olds, with very low master's attainment (2.1% among 25‑64 year‑olds) . The inverse relationship is clear: lower educational attainment correlates with higher fertility.

4. **Historical OECD data (1997)** document that Italian long university degrees (the traditional five‑year *laurea*) were completed at median ages between **25 and 29 years** , reflecting a structural feature that persists in modified form today.

5. **The "speed of modernization" thesis** advanced by Goldin (2024) and discussed in earlier analyses provides the interpretive framework: countries like Italy that experienced rapid post‑war modernization without corresponding adaptation of gender roles and institutional structures show lower fertility, even when educational attainment levels are not the highest among OECD countries .

## 1. Master's Degree Attainment Across OECD Countries: OECD Education at a Glance 2025

### 1.1 OECD Average Master's Attainment

According to *Education at a Glance 2025*, the share of 25‑34 year‑olds with a master's or equivalent degree as their highest level of education varies widely across OECD countries . Key data points:

| Country | Master's Degree Attainment (25‑34 year‑olds, 2024) | Total Fertility Rate (2025) |
|---------|---------------------------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Luxembourg | **39%** (highest in OECD)  | ~1.3 |
| OECD Average | **16%**  | 1.46 |
| Finland | **15%**  | ~1.4 |
| Italy | **12.9%** (among 25‑64) | 1.2 |
| Australia | **11%**  | 1.6 |
| Mexico | **2.1%** (among 25‑64)  | 1.9 |
| Türkiye | **2.1%** (among 25‑64)  | ~1.8 |

*Sources: OECD Education at a Glance 2025 ; Luxembourg Times ; Australia country note ; OECD TFR data from Pensions at a Glance 2025 [previous analysis]*

### 1.2 The Luxembourg Exception

Luxembourg represents the extreme end of master's attainment, with **39%** of 25‑34 year‑olds holding a master's degree—more than double the OECD average of 16% . Yet Luxembourg's TFR is approximately 1.3, comparable to Italy's. This suggests that **master's attainment alone does not determine fertility outcomes**; other factors (migration, institutional context, gender norms) mediate the relationship.

### 1.3 The Korean Contrast

Korea presents a different pattern: **70.6%** of 25‑34 year‑olds hold tertiary qualifications (the highest in the OECD), yet the share with master's degrees is only **5.5%** among 25‑64 year‑olds . Korea's TFR is the lowest in the OECD at 0.7. This suggests that **high bachelor's attainment combined with relatively low master's attainment** does not prevent very low fertility—other factors (gender inequality, housing costs, labor market structure) dominate.

## 2. Age at Graduation: Historical OECD Data and Persistent Patterns

### 2.1 OECD 1997 Data on Graduation Ages

A comprehensive analysis from the 1997 OECD report (cited in the *RoutledgeFalmer Reader in Higher Education*) documents the average age at graduation across countries :

> "The median age ranges for short university‑level degrees from about 22 in the UK to about 26 in Finland and Sweden; for long university degrees between 25 and 28; and for second university degrees (e.g. master subsequent to bachelor) from about 25 to about 30."

Specifically for long university programmes (the traditional *laurea* system in Italy, which combined bachelor's and master's‑level study):

| Country | 25th Percentile Age | 75th Percentile Age |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Netherlands (long programmes) | 24 years | 28 years |
| Norway (long programmes) | 24 years | 28 years |
| **Italy (long programmes)** | **25 years** | **29 years** |
| Austria (long programmes) | 26 years | 30 years |
| Finland (long programmes) | 26 years | 30 years |

### 2.2 Persistence of the Pattern

While the Bologna Process (initiated in 1999) reformed European higher education into a three‑year bachelor's + two‑year master's structure, the underlying pattern of **delayed graduation** persists in Italy for several reasons:

1. **Low completion rates**: Italian students take longer than the theoretical duration to complete degrees
2. **Delayed enrollment**: Many Italian students enter university later than peers in other OECD countries
3. **Part‑time study**: Significant numbers of students work while studying, extending time to degree
4. **Field of study effects**: Certain fields (engineering, architecture, medicine) have longer theoretical durations

The Finnish case illustrates this: despite having a master's attainment rate (15%) similar to Italy's, Finland maintains higher fertility (~1.4 vs Italy's 1.2). The Chinese‑language summary of *Education at a Glance 2025* notes:

> "芬兰的平均入学年龄较晚以及入学前学生休'间隔年'（gap year）的现象非常普遍" — "Finland's average enrollment age is later and the phenomenon of students taking 'gap years' before enrollment is very common" .

This suggests that **late graduation alone does not determine fertility outcomes**; the *nature* of the transition from education to employment and the *availability of institutional support* for combining work and family matter more.

## 3. Comparative Analysis: Italy vs. Higher‑TFR Countries

### 3.1 The Mexican Case: Low Education, Higher Fertility

Mexico offers the clearest illustration of the inverse education‑fertility relationship:

| Indicator | Mexico | OECD Average | Italy |
|-----------|--------|--------------|-------|
| Tertiary attainment (25‑34) | 27.06% | 48% | 28.27% |
| Master's attainment (25‑64) | 2.1% | 13.6% | 12.9% |
| TFR (2025) | 1.9 | 1.46 | 1.2 |

Mexico's TFR is significantly higher than both the OECD average and Italy's, while its educational attainment is substantially lower. This supports the hypothesis that **extended education postpones childbearing**—in Mexico, where fewer young people pursue higher education, family formation occurs earlier.

### 3.2 The French Case: Moderate Education, Higher Fertility

France, which the user identified in the initial prompt as a country maintaining higher fertility among wealthy, historically Christian populations, presents a different profile:

| Indicator | France | Italy |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Tertiary attainment (25‑34) | 50.26% | 28.27% |
| TFR (2025) | ~1.7 | 1.2 |

France has **significantly higher** tertiary attainment than Italy, yet maintains **higher fertility**. This demonstrates that high educational attainment does not inevitably lead to very low fertility. The difference lies in:

1. **Gender equality**: France has smaller gender care gaps and more extensive childcare provision
2. **Labor market structure**: More stable employment trajectories for young people
3. **Housing policies**: Greater availability of affordable housing
4. **Cultural norms**: Less intensive parenting expectations (or different expression of them)

### 3.3 The Finnish Case: Late Graduation, Moderate Fertility

Finland provides a particularly instructive comparison:

| Indicator | Finland | Italy |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Tertiary attainment (25‑34) | 39% | 28.27% |
| Master's attainment (25‑34) | 15% | 12.9% (25‑64) |
| Average enrollment age | Later (gap years common) | Relatively early |
| TFR (2025) | ~1.4 | 1.2 |

Finland's TFR is higher than Italy's despite similar master's attainment and later enrollment ages. The Finnish advantage likely derives from:
- Strong institutional support for families (extensive childcare, parental leave)
- More egalitarian gender norms
- Better labor market integration of graduates
- Lower housing costs relative to income

## 4. The "Speed of Modernization" Interpretation

### 4.1 Goldin's Framework Applied to Education Timing

The user's initial prompt referenced Goldin's categorization of countries by speed of modernization. This framework applies directly to education timing:

**Gradual modernizers (France, Germany, Sweden, UK, US)** : 
- Educational expansion occurred incrementally over generations
- Gender norms adapted alongside educational change
- Institutional supports evolved in parallel
- Result: higher fertility (1.7‑1.8)

**Rapid modernizers (Italy, Japan, Korea, Spain)** :
- Educational expansion occurred rapidly after WWII
- Gender norms did not adapt at the same pace
- Institutional supports lagged behind educational change
- Result: lower fertility (1.2‑1.3)

In this framework, the *speed* of educational expansion—not simply the *level*—matters. Italy's transformation from a largely agrarian society with low educational attainment to a modern economy with widespread tertiary education occurred in a compressed timeframe (roughly 1960‑1995), leaving traditional gender roles and family structures intact while women entered the workforce.

### 4.2 Education Timing and the Fertility Window

The structural feature of Italian education—delayed graduation ages combined with precarious labor market entry—creates a compressed "fertility window":

```
Age 19: Enter university (later than many OECD peers)
Age 24‑26: Complete bachelor's degree (often later than theoretical duration)
Age 26‑28: Complete master's degree (if pursued)
Age 28‑32: Achieve stable employment
Age 32+: First child (if possible)
```

This compressed window (approximately ages 28‑38) leaves limited time for multiple children, and any additional delay (economic uncertainty, housing insecurity, partner search) reduces the likelihood of reaching desired family size.

## 7. Conclusions

1. **Italy's master's degree attainment rate is 12.9% among 25‑64 year‑olds**, close to the OECD average of 13.6% but below the 16% average for 25‑34 year‑olds .

2. **Historical OECD data (1997) document that Italian long university degrees were completed at median ages of 25‑29 years**, a pattern that has partially persisted through the Bologna Process .

3. **The inverse education‑fertility relationship is clearly visible**: Mexico (27% tertiary attainment, 1.9 TFR) and Korea (71% tertiary attainment, 0.7 TFR) represent the extremes .

4. **France demonstrates that high educational attainment does not inevitably lead to very low fertility**: with 50% tertiary attainment and a TFR of ~1.7, France outperforms both Italy and the OECD average .

5. **The "speed of modernization" framework explains cross‑country variation**: rapid educational expansion in Italy without corresponding adaptation of gender roles and institutional supports creates the conditions for delayed fertility .

6. **Education timing matters more than education level**: Italy's relatively low tertiary attainment (28%) combined with very late first births (32.2 years, from previous analyses) suggests that the *structure* of educational pathways—delayed completion ages—compresses the fertility window and reduces the time available for multiple children.

7. **Policy implications**: Countries seeking to raise fertility while maintaining high educational attainment should focus on:
   - Reducing the duration of educational pathways where possible
   - Ensuring smooth transitions from education to stable employment
   - Providing institutional supports (childcare, parental leave) that allow women to combine careers and families
   - Addressing gender inequality in unpaid care work
