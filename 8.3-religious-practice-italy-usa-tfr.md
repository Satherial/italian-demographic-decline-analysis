# Analysis Point 8 (Cross-Country Comparison): Religious Practice and Fertility in Italy vs. United States
*Comparative Analysis by Age Group and Correlation with TFR – Data from NSFG, EVS, and World Family Map*

## Executive Summary

This analysis compares the level of religious practice between Italy and the United States across age groups and examines the correlation with total fertility rates (TFR). The available data reveal a consistent pattern: **higher religious practice is associated with higher fertility in both countries**, but the magnitude of the correlation varies significantly, and Italy's fertility remains low even among its most devout populations.

Key findings from the available sources:

1. **Religious importance**: 59% of U.S. adults report that religion is "very important" in their lives, compared to 27% in Italy (Pew 2002 data) —a more than twofold difference .

2. **Fertility by religious practice (U.S.)**: Among U.S. Catholic women aged 18-44, those attending church more than once weekly have a mean of **1.78 children**, compared to **1.26 children** among those who never attend. Women with no religious affiliation and no attendance have **1.00 children** .

3. **Fertility by religious practice (Europe)**: Among European Catholics (EVS 2000), those attending more than once weekly have a mean of **1.81 children**, compared to **1.09 children** among non‑attenders—a similar pattern but with lower baseline fertility .

4. **The Italian anomaly**: Despite having higher religious importance than other European countries (27% vs. France's 11% and Germany's 21%), Italy's TFR remains among the lowest in Europe (1.2 in 2024). This suggests that **religious practice alone does not guarantee higher fertility**; structural and cultural factors mediate the relationship .

5. **Contemporary global evidence**: The 2019 World Family Map reports that in low-fertility countries, individuals who attend religious services regularly have **0.27 more children** than those who never attend—a statistically significant but modest difference .

6. **Age group data limitation**: Specific age-group breakdowns for Italy (beyond the 18-44 category) were not available in the search results; however, the NSFG U.S. data for women aged 18-44 provide a benchmark for childbearing years.

These findings support the hypothesis that religious practice is positively correlated with fertility, but the correlation is **weaker in Italy than in the United States**, and Italy's exceptionally low fertility persists even among its most devout populations—suggesting that secularization is one factor among many.

## 1. Overall Religious Importance: Italy vs. United States

### 1.1 Pew Global Attitudes Survey (2002)

A Pew poll cited in the First Things analysis (2009) provides a benchmark comparison of religious importance across countries :

| Country | Adults for whom religion is "very important" |
|---------|---------------------------------------------|
| United States | **59%** |
| Poland | 36% |
| Italy | **27%** |
| Great Britain | 33% |
| Germany | 21% |
| France | 11% |

The United States stands out as an outlier among wealthy nations, with a level of religious importance nearly double that of Italy—and Italy itself has higher religious importance than other major Western European countries .

### 1.2 Interpretation

This 2002 data suggests that Italy occupies a **middle position** in the European context: more religious than France or Germany, but substantially less religious than the United States. However, as documented in previous analyses, Italian religious practice has declined significantly since 2002, with weekly mass attendance falling from 36.4% in 2001 to 17.9% in 2023 [previous analysis]. The 2002 data therefore likely **overstates** current Italian religious importance relative to the United States.

## 2. Fertility by Religious Practice: United States Data

### 2.1 National Survey of Family Growth (NSFG 2002)

The NSFG (2002) provides detailed data on the correlation between religious practice and number of children among U.S. women aged 18-44 :

| Religious Group / Practice | Mean Number of Children |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Self-identified Catholics, attend >1x/week | **1.78** |
| Self-identified Catholics, never attend | **1.26** |
| No denomination, never attend | **1.00** |

### 2.2 Additional NSFG Findings

The same source notes that **12% of American women aged 18-44 attend church more than once a week** —a substantial cohort of highly devout women of childbearing age .

### 2.3 Interpretation

The NSFG data reveal a clear gradient: more frequent religious attendance correlates with higher fertility. Among U.S. Catholic women of childbearing age, those attending more than once weekly have **0.52 more children** (approximately 40% higher) than those who never attend.

## 3. Fertility by Religious Practice: European Data

### 3.1 European Values Study (EVS 2000)

The European Values Study (EVS 2000) provides comparable data for European Catholics :

| Practice Level | Mean Number of Children (European Catholics) |
|----------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Attend >1x/week | **1.81** |
| Never attend | **1.09** |

The pattern mirrors the United States: more frequent attendance correlates with higher fertility. The difference (0.72 children) is even slightly larger than in the U.S. data, though the baseline fertility is comparable.

### 3.2 The Italian Anomaly Within Europe

The First Things analysis notes a crucial caveat :

> "When Frejka and Westhoff look at individual European countries and regions, the picture gets muddy. Norwegians, it seems, have children in spite of religious disinterest. Italians don’t have children even if they go to church."

This observation—that **Italian Catholics who attend church still have very low fertility**—is supported by Italy's persistently low TFR despite its relatively higher religious importance compared to other European countries.

## 4. Contemporary Global Evidence: World Family Map 2019

### 4.1 Key Findings

The 2019 World Family Map, produced by the Institute for Family Studies and the Wheatley Institute, provides more recent global data on religion and fertility :

> "Religion’s positive influence on fertility has become stronger in recent decades, according to data from low-fertility countries in the Americas, East Asia, and Europe. Today, people (ages 18-49) who attend religious services regularly have **0.27 more children** than those who never, or practically never, attend."

### 4.2 The Marriage Channel

The report identifies **marriage** as an important mediating mechanism :

> "Marriage plays an important role in explaining religion’s continued positive influence on childbearing because religious men and women are more likely to marry compared to their more secular peers, and the married have more children than the unmarried."

This finding suggests that the fertility advantage of religious individuals operates partly through higher marriage rates.

### 4.3 Country Coverage

The World Family Map 2019 covers 11 countries: Argentina, Australia, Canada, Chile, Colombia, France, Ireland, Mexico, Peru, the United Kingdom, and the United States . Italy is not included in this specific analysis, but the findings for low-fertility countries (which include France and the UK) are relevant.

## 5. Comparative Analysis: Italy vs. United States

### 5.1 Available Comparative Data

| Indicator | Italy | United States |
|-----------|-------|---------------|
| Religion "very important" (2002) | 27% | 59% |
| Catholic weekly mass attendance (2023/2002) | 17.9% (2023) | Data not available for 2002 |
| TFR (2024/2002) | 1.2 (2024) | 2.01 (2002) |
| Highly religious women (18-44) attending >1x/week | Data not available | 12% |

### 5.2 Patterns Observed

1. **Higher religious importance in the U.S.** : The 2002 data show a large gap, likely larger today given Italy's accelerating secularization since 2002 .

2. **Higher fertility in the U.S.** : The U.S. TFR was near replacement (2.01) in 2002; today it is approximately 1.6–1.7, still substantially above Italy's 1.2.

3. **The Italian paradox**: Despite higher religious importance than France or Germany, Italy's fertility is among Europe's lowest. This suggests that **religious practice alone cannot explain cross-country fertility differences**.

### 5.3 Possible Explanations for the Italian Paradox

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Structural barriers** | Italy's labor market precarity, housing crisis, and gender care gap affect all women regardless of religiosity |
| **Institutional weakness** | Italian parish communities may provide less practical support for families than their American counterparts |
| **Cultural Catholicism** | Many Italians identify as Catholic "culturally" without internalizing pronatalist teachings |
| **Gender roles** | Traditional gender roles persist in Italy, creating conflict for educated women regardless of religious commitment |

## 7. Synthesis: Religion, Fertility, and the Italian Case

### 7.1 The Correlation Is Real but Modest

The available evidence supports a modest positive correlation between religious practice and fertility:

- In the U.S., highly devout Catholic women have 0.52 more children than non-attenders
- In Europe, highly devout Catholic women have 0.72 more children than non-attenders
- Across low-fertility countries, regular attenders have 0.27 more children (World Family Map 2019)

### 7.2 The Italian Paradox

Italy presents a puzzle: despite higher religious importance than France or Germany, its fertility remains lower. Several factors may explain this:

1. **The "cultural Catholic" phenomenon**: Many Italians identify as Catholic but do not practice, and this identification does not translate into pronatalist behavior
2. **Structural barriers outweigh religious effects**: Economic precarity, housing costs, and gender inequality affect even devout Italians
3. **Institutional weakness**: Italian parishes may not provide the same level of family support as American religious communities
4. **Generational rupture**: The collapse of religious practice among young Italians (only 58.3% identify as Catholic, 10.9% practice) means that the pro-fertility effect of religion is concentrated in older generations

### 7.3 Implications for the Broader Analysis

This cross-country comparison supports several points in the user's framework:

1. **Secularization matters**: The U.S., with higher religious practice, has higher fertility
2. **But secularization is not the only factor**: Italy's fertility remains low despite being more religious than France or Germany
3. **Structural factors mediate**: The Italian context—labor market, housing, gender roles—amplifies the fertility-depressing effects of secularization

## 9. Conclusions

1. **Religious importance is higher in the United States than in Italy**: 59% of U.S. adults consider religion "very important" compared to 27% in Italy (2002 data) .

2. **A positive correlation exists between religious practice and fertility**: In both the U.S. and Europe, Catholics attending more than once weekly have approximately 0.5–0.7 more children than non-attenders .

3. **The Italian paradox**: Despite higher religious importance than other Western European countries, Italy's TFR is among the lowest in Europe, suggesting that religious practice alone cannot explain cross-country fertility differences .

4. **Structural factors mediate the religion-fertility relationship**: Italy's labor market precarity, housing crisis, and gender care gap likely offset any pro-fertility effect of religious practice .

5. **Age-specific data for Italy are limited**: The available sources do not provide detailed age-group breakdowns for Italian religious practice and fertility comparable to U.S. NSFG data .

6. **Contemporary global evidence confirms a modest effect**: The 2019 World Family Map found that in low-fertility countries, regular religious attenders have 0.27 more children than non-attenders .

7. **The generational rupture in Italy is critical**: With only 10.9% of young Italian Catholics attending services regularly, the pro-fertility effect of religion is increasingly concentrated in older cohorts and may continue to weaken .
