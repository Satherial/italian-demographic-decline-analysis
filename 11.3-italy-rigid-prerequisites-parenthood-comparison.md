## The American Exception: Risk Tolerance, Individualism, and Religious Community

- Document the centrality of homeownership as a prerequisite for parenthood in Italy through recent empirical research. Using longitudinal data from the Bank of Italy's Survey on Household Income and Wealth (1998–2016), Gallo and Vignoli found that higher housing expenditure is positively associated with childbearing only among homeowners, not among tenants. This finding demonstrates that "property ownership and improved housing conditions are perceived as essential prerequisites for parenthood in Italy." Homeownership—"even if it leads to a financial burden—represents a crucial milestone in the childbearing process, at least in Italy" .

- Analyze the structural transformation of the Italian housing market that reinforced homeownership as a cultural imperative. In the late 1970s, homeownership and renting were relatively balanced at 51% and 41% respectively. By 2008, homeownership had increased to 70%, while rental rates dropped to 21%. Recent Eurostat data shows that as of 2021, 74% of Italians own their homes and only 18% rent. This shift was initially facilitated by mass homeownership through self-construction and loose regulation, but after 1990, tighter building regulations increased barriers to housing access for young cohorts, forcing them to increasingly rely on parental resources to purchase homes .

- Examine the financial strain of housing as a barrier to family formation. According to Istat data, 9% of Italian households spend at least 40% of their monthly income on housing. Additionally, 16.3% of renting households are in arrears on rent payments, while 9.5% struggle to pay mortgage installments. Despite these burdens, the cultural imperative to own before having children persists. The positive association between housing expenditure and childbearing among homeowners suggests that "people are willing to invest in housing prior to childbearing," yet this relationship holds exclusively for wealthier households who can sustain both higher housing costs and the financial demands of raising children .

- Document the macroeconomic paradox of homeownership and fertility in Italy. While micro-level evidence suggests homeownership facilitates the transition to parenthood, cross-national research shows that countries with high homeownership rates often have low birth rates. Italy exemplifies this paradox: "owning a home before starting a family is very common, and renting is not seen as a real option." This strong desire to own before having children is "tied to social norms in Italy" and results in an "anticipation effect"—couples purchase homes before becoming parents, which can delay family formation and "consequently lead to a decline in overall fertility rates" .

- Analyze the persistence of marriage as a prerequisite for parenthood in Italy despite broader European trends. Unlike Northwestern Europe, where cohabitation has become an accepted alternative to marriage, "marriage in Italy has maintained a crucial role in the process of family formation." While cohabitation is spreading among younger generations, it spreads "without a questioning of the conjugal basis of family" and "in continuity with some strong peculiarities of the Mediterranean family pattern." Marriage continues to represent the prevalent form of union, and parenthood is placed almost solely within it .

- Examine the role of intergenerational ties in reinforcing these prerequisites. Research from Sardinia shows that "strong ties among kin-related women represent the principal resource for material and psychological support in daily and occasional circumstances" and serve as "major vehicles for the socialization of young women." Conversely, women isolated from maternal kin "are more likely to eschew the double presence model to welcome the first serious marriage prospect, thereby abandoning their educational pattern too early to have reasonable expectations of employment and devoting themselves to family and children." Additionally, "the isolation of a nuclear family may also hinder family formation for young women who have sole responsibility for ageing and dependent parents" .

- Analyze the delayed entry into independent living that accompanies these prerequisites. OECD data shows that 79% of Italians under 30 live with their parents, placing Italy among the highest rates in the developed world alongside South Korea (82%). In contrast, in Norway and Sweden the rate is 22%, in Finland 14%, and in Denmark 12%. France reports 44%, Germany 33%, and the United States 34%. Only one in five Italians under 30 maintains independent residence . This prolonged cohabitation is increasingly recognized as structural rather than cultural: the Coordinamento Nazionale Docenti della disciplina dei Diritti Umani emphasizes that "non sono bamboccioni, è una crisi strutturale" (they are not "big babies," it is a structural crisis) .

- Document the uncertainty that young Italians face regarding housing autonomy. Among 18-24 year olds, 60% fear they will not be able to find adequate housing within the next two years. This insecurity is not merely economic but existential: "senza una casa non si costruisce autonomia, non si pianifica un progetto di vita e non si esercita pienamente la cittadinanza" (without a home, one cannot build autonomy, plan a life project, or fully exercise citizenship) .

- Contrast the Italian prerequisites with the American approach to risk and instability in family formation. American young adults report a "pretty strong set of prerequisites" for having children: finishing school, securing a decent job with decent income, establishing a good partnership, and living independently (Pew Research Center, 2024). However, unlike the Italian context where these prerequisites are rigidly tied to homeownership and permanent employment, American culture demonstrates greater flexibility in how these conditions are met. The average age of first-time homebuyers has risen to 38—five years older than just five years ago—yet Americans continue to form families while renting, with 72% of renters now age 30 or older, an all-time high (Realtor.com, 2025). This suggests a cultural tolerance for family formation under less-than-ideal housing conditions.

- Identify the structural consequences of Italy's rigid prerequisites for intergenerational support. The Italian model places substantial demands on extended family networks. Young adults rely on parental resources for home purchases , and women depend on maternal kin for material and psychological support . This reliance reinforces the prerequisites: young people cannot form families without first securing the conditions that demonstrate their readiness within this interdependent system.

- Compare the cultural logic underlying fertility decline in Italy and the United States. Italian decline reflects what demographers term the "defense of existing lifestyle"—a risk-averse culture that views parenthood as threatening to established comfort and leisure, with rigid prerequisites (homeownership, permanent employment, marriage) that must be met before family formation can proceed. American decline, by contrast, reflects a combination of structural barriers (housing costs, childcare expenses) and a cultural shift toward individual fulfillment goals that compete with family formation. American individualism enables greater flexibility: Americans change jobs, move across regions, and adapt living situations to accommodate family formation in ways that Italians, with stronger ties to place and family, find more difficult. The "making it work" ethos—the belief that one can overcome external barriers through individual effort—remains a distinctive feature of American cultural psychology (Routledge, Harvard Kennedy School, 2024).
